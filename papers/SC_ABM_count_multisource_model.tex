\title{SC-ABM: A Hierarchical Count-Based Spatio-Temporal Agent-Based Model with Multi-Source Observation}

\author{Xulin Pan\corref{cor1}}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}
\ead{xxp5066@psu.edu}
\address{Pennsylvania State University,
         University Park, PA 16802, USA}

\begin{abstract}
We study variational inference for a class of spatio-temporal count
agent-based models (SC-ABM) in which the latent abundance field evolves
through a Ricker-type Binomial survival, Poisson neighbourhood immigration,
and Poisson long-distance arrivals, observed through NegBin count and Bernoulli
remote-sensing channels.  The exact posterior is intractable because the
transition density is a Binomial--Poisson convolution with no closed-form
log-PMF, making the evidence lower bound (ELBO) --- the standard variational
objective --- analytically inaccessible.

We show that the Poisson moment generating function (MGF) converts the
KL-minimisation problem into a tractable form: under a Poisson mean-field
family, the survival expectation, binary detection probability, and immigration
rate are all obtained analytically (Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}), defining a
surrogate ELBO with three disclosed approximation sources each of bounded order.
We prove that the resulting structured mean-field VB (SMFVB) coordinate-ascent
sequence is monotonically non-decreasing and converges to a stationary point
geometrically, at a rate $\rho\le1/2$ determined by the same Foster--Lyapunov
drift condition that guarantees ergodicity of the latent chain
(Theorem~\ref{thm:vi-converge}).
The first-order optimality condition of the surrogate KL problem yields a
\emph{multiplicatively corrected forward pass} of the model's conditional
mean recursion --- the Bayesian analogue of the Kalman prediction--correction
step for this nonlinear, non-Gaussian, discrete-state model.

In simulation (100 replicates, $m=100$, $T=15$), SMFVB achieves 95\%
credible interval coverage within 4 percentage points of particle MCMC
and posterior mean discrepancies below 3\%, with a $22\times$ wall-time
reduction ($4$ min vs.\ $92$ min).  The 2--4~pp coverage shortfall
is theoretically predicted from the mean-field Binomial-survival
underestimation and is empirically confirmed.
Applied to \textit{Coquillettidia perturbans} mosquito surveillance at NEON
UNDE, a constant-LDD fit tracks summer emergence but fails to reproduce the
autumn collapse, providing a clean diagnostic demonstration of the PP check.
A minimal seasonal extension $\psi_t=\psi\exp\{-\frac{1}{2}((t-\mu_s)/\sigma_s)^2\}$,
fitted with two additional parameters and a peak-timing summary statistic,
recovers the full bell-curve trajectory and yields ecologically interpretable
posterior estimates of emergence peak ($\hat\mu_s$) and season width ($\hat\sigma_s$).
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

Many spreading phenomena are observed neither perfectly nor as simple binary
presence--absence states. Ecological abundance, invasive species expansion, disease
incidence, fire occurrence, and animal movement often involve:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item unobserved latent abundance,
    \item imperfect detection,
    \item heterogeneous observation sources,
    \item spatially structured local dispersal,
    \item long-distance colonization, and
    \item density-dependent persistence or growth.
\end{enumerate}

The purpose of SC-ABM is to provide a count-valued hierarchical agent-based model
that links these mechanisms in one coherent statistical framework.
SC-ABM extends the statistical agent-based model (ABM) framework of
\citet{hooten2010} and the likelihood-free inference developments of
\citet{heard2014}, replacing binary presence--absence states with continuous
latent count fields and multi-source observation likelihoods.
The multi-source fusion of imperfect count surveys and binary remote-sensing
data follows the integrated data model approach of \citet{verhoef2025}.

The closest existing framework is the N-mixture model of Royle (2004) and its
dynamic extension \citep[Dail and Madsen 2011]{dail2011}, which model imperfect
detection of a latent integer-valued abundance.  SC-ABM generalises N-mixture
models in three directions: (i) the transition kernel is a mechanistic
agent-based dispersal model rather than a simple binomial removal and Poisson
recruitment; (ii) two heterogeneous observation channels with different detection
physics are fused over a shared latent field; and (iii) the spatial structure is
explicit through neighbourhood dispersal and a Gaussian process habitat prior.
Unlike closed-population models, SC-ABM accommodates open population dynamics
with density regulation, neighbourhood connectivity, and long-distance arrivals.

\paragraph{Main contribution.}
The central contribution of this paper is the SMFVB algorithm
(\S\ref{sec:vi}): a coordinate-ascent variational inference scheme for SC-ABM
that exploits the Poisson MGF to derive exact closed-form ELBO terms for an
otherwise intractable Binomial--Poisson transition density.
The SC-ABM model (\S\S\ref{sec:process}--\ref{sec:obs}) is the \emph{vehicle}
on which SMFVB operates; its components (Ricker survival, multinomial-logit
dispersal with self-retention, Gaussian process habitat prior, dual-channel
observation) are design choices motivated by ecological realism, not claimed as
independent novel contributions.
The convergence theorem, approximation-quality corollary, and
model-selection criterion (\S\S\ref{sec:vi-convergence}--\ref{sec:elbo-sel})
are theoretical properties that follow from SMFVB.
Local identifiability of the dual-channel observation model
(Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}, \S\ref{sec:identif-val}) is a
statistical prerequisite for the posterior consistency claim.
The ABC-SMC comparator (\S\ref{sec:abc}) is a likelihood-free baseline
used to benchmark SMFVB in the simulation study; it is not a contribution of
this paper.
The NEON application (\S\ref{sec:neon}) is an illustrative analysis
conducted with ABC-SMC and serves to demonstrate the model's ecological
interpretability, not to validate SMFVB.

As a concrete illustration, we apply SC-ABM in \S\ref{sec:neon} to
\textit{Coquillettidia perturbans} mosquito surveillance at the NEON site UNDE
(Underhill Forest, Michigan), where overnight CDC trap counts ($Y^{(C)}$) and
daytime binary detections ($Y^{(P)}$) from $m=10$ plots over 16 biweekly steps
are fused under the shared latent abundance model.

\section{Latent Process Model}\label{sec:process}

The latent count evolves through three additive components:

\[
N_{i,t}=S_{i,t}+I_{i,t}+L_{i,t},\qquad t=2,\ldots,T.
\]

Conditional on the previous latent field \(\bm N_{t-1}=(N_{1,t-1},\ldots,N_{m,t-1})\),
the three components are assumed conditionally independent.

\subsection{Density-Regulated Local Persistence}

Surviving individuals are modeled as

\[
S_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t-1},\phi_i,K_i
\sim
\mathrm{Binomial}\left(N_{i,t-1},p^S_{i,t-1}\right),
\]

where

\[
p^S_{i,t-1}
=
\phi_i\exp\left(-\frac{N_{i,t-1}}{K_i}\right).
\]

This is a Ricker-type density-regulated persistence model \citep{turchin1998}.
Empirical evidence that density-dependent dispersal and survival improve near-term
range-shift forecasts over climate-only models is provided by \citet{pletcher2025}.
When \(N_{i,t-1}\ll K_i\),

\[
p^S_{i,t-1}\approx \phi_i,
\]

whereas large local abundance reduces persistence through density pressure. The
conditional survival mean is

\[
\E[S_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t-1}]
=
N_{i,t-1}\phi_i
\exp\left(-\frac{N_{i,t-1}}{K_i}\right).
\]

The baseline persistence and carrying-capacity scale may be modeled as

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\phi_i)=x_i^\top\beta_\phi,
\]

and

\[
\log K_i=x_i^\top\beta_K+u_i,\qquad u_i\sim N(0,\sigma_K^2).
\]

\subsection{Neighborhood Immigration}

For each source cell \(j\), define the dispersal probability from source
\(j\) to neighbouring target \(i\in\mathcal{N}_j\) via a multinomial logit
with an explicit \emph{self-retention} baseline:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:dispersal}
p_{j\to i}
=
\frac{\exp\{\theta_0-\theta_d d_{ji}+\theta_h(\alpha_i-\alpha_j)
+\theta_x^\top(x_i-x_j)\}}
{1+\sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}_j}
\exp\{\theta_0-\theta_d d_{jk}+\theta_h(\alpha_k-\alpha_j)
+\theta_x^\top(x_k-x_j)\}}.
\end{equation}

Here \(d_{ji}\) is the Euclidean distance between centroids of source \(j\)
and target \(i\), and \(\alpha_i-\alpha_j\) is the habitat-suitability gradient
driving uphill dispersal.  The parameter \(\theta_h\) (habitat gradient weight)
is distinct from the GP range \(\theta_\alpha\) in~\S\ref{sec:spatial}.
The constant \(1\) in the denominator represents the baseline
(staying in cell \(j\)); the self-retention probability is
\[
  p_{j\to j} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j} p_{j\to i} \;\in(0,1).
\]
Unlike a pure-multinomial normalisation, this ensures
\(\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j} p_{j\to i} < 1\) for all neighbourhood
configurations, accommodating the realistic case where most individuals
remain in their natal cell.

The expected dispersal pressure from source \(j\) to target \(i\) is

\[
\lambda_{j\to i,t}
=
\omega_j p_{j\to i}
\frac{N_{j,t-1}}{N_{j,t-1}+h},
\]

where \(\omega_j>0\) is the maximum expected local dispersal pressure from source
cell \(j\), and \(h>0\) is a half-saturation parameter. The total neighborhood
immigration rate into cell \(i\) is

\[
\Lambda_{i,t}
=
\sum_{j:\, i\in\mathcal{N}_j}
\lambda_{j\to i,t}.
\]

Then

\[
I_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1},\alpha,\Theta
\sim
\mathrm{Poisson}(\Lambda_{i,t}).
\]

This Poisson-superposition formulation allows several source cells to contribute
additively to the same target cell.
\citet{whitehouse2023} show that Poisson Approximate Likelihoods (PAL) for
stochastic compartmental models---which share this additive Poisson
superposition structure---yield consistent and computationally efficient
inference, providing theoretical support for the Poisson immigration channel.

\subsection{Long-Distance Arrivals}

Long-distance dispersal, immigration, or background colonization is modeled as

\[
L_{i,t}\mid \psi_i\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\psi_i),
\]

with

\begin{equation}\label{eq:ldd}
\log\psi_i=w_i^\top\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi,
\end{equation}

where \(w_i\) contains long-range connectivity covariates (road proximity,
river proximity, wind corridor, etc.) and \(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi\) is a
coefficient vector.  The symbol \(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi\) is used
throughout to avoid confusion with the remote-sensing false-positive
probability \(\eta_{i,t}\) in~\S\ref{sec:obs}.

\subsection{Conditional Moments}

Because \(S_{i,t}\), \(I_{i,t}\), and \(L_{i,t}\) are conditionally independent,

\[
\E[N_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1}]
=
N_{i,t-1}p^S_{i,t-1}+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i.
\]

The conditional variance is

\[
\Var(N_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1})
=
N_{i,t-1}p^S_{i,t-1}(1-p^S_{i,t-1})
+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i.
\]

Thus the process model naturally generates extra-Poisson variation because the
latent count is a convolution of a binomial survival term and two Poisson arrival
terms.
The spatiotemporal count structure of SC-ABM parallels the penalized Poisson
framework of \citet{zhang2025csda}, who develop a doubly-fused penalization
scheme for simultaneously detecting temporal change points and spatial clusters
in count data, and show that doubly-penalized maximum likelihood estimators are
consistent under suitable regularity conditions.

\subsection{Stochastic Stability}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric ergodicity of the single-cell chain]
\label{thm:ergodicity}
Consider a single isolated cell ($\mathcal{N}_i=\emptyset$) with dynamics
\[
  N_t \;=\; S_t + L_t,
  \quad
  S_t \mid N_{t-1} \sim \mathrm{Binomial}\!\left(N_{t-1},\,\phi e^{-N_{t-1}/K}\right),
  \quad
  L_t \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\psi),
\]
where $\phi\in(0,1)$, $K>0$, and $\psi\ge 0$.  Then $\{N_t\}_{t\ge 0}$ is
a geometrically ergodic Markov chain on $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ possessing a
unique stationary distribution $\pi_\phi$ with $\E_{\pi_\phi}[N]\le\phi K/e+\psi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply the Foster--Lyapunov drift criterion \citep{meyntweedie1993} with $V(n)=n$.
Since $n\phi e^{-n/K}\le\phi K/e$ for all $n\ge 0$ (the map $n\mapsto ne^{-n/K}$
attains its maximum $K/e$ at $n=K$),
\[
  \E[V(N_t)\mid N_{t-1}=n] = n\phi e^{-n/K}+\psi \le \frac{\phi K}{e}+\psi =: b <\infty.
\]
The condition $\phi e^{-n/K}<1/2$ is equivalent to $n > K\log(2\phi)$,
so define $n^* := \max\!\bigl(0,\; K\log(2\phi)\bigr)$.  For $n>n^*$ we have
$\phi e^{-n/K}<1/2$, so
$\E[N_t\mid N_{t-1}=n]\le n/2+\psi$.  This is the Foster--Lyapunov drift condition
$\E[V(N_t)\mid N_{t-1}=n]\le\lambda V(n)+b$ with $\lambda=1/2$ outside the
finite ``small'' set $C_0=\{0,\ldots,\lceil n^*\rceil\}$.
(When $\phi\le1/2$, $n^*=0$ and the condition holds for all $n\ge 1$.)
Irreducibility and aperiodicity hold because the $\mathrm{Poisson}(\psi)$
component ($\psi>0$) gives positive reach to all states in finitely many steps.
Geometric ergodicity follows from \citet{meyntweedie1993}, Theorem~15.0.1.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional mean bound under normalized dispersal]
\label{prop:bound}
On the full $m$-cell grid, suppose $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}=1$ for
all $j$ (standard softmax normalization) and let $\phi_{\max}=\max_i\phi_i<1$,
$\Psi=\sum_i\psi_i$.  Then
\[
  \E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m N_{i,t}\;\middle|\;\bm{N}_{t-1}\right]
  \;\le\;
  \phi_{\max}\sum_{i=1}^m N_{i,t-1}
  + \sum_{j=1}^m \frac{\omega_j N_{j,t-1}}{N_{j,t-1}+h}
  + \Psi.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By conditional independence, $\E[\sum_i N_{i,t}\mid\bm{N}_{t-1}]
= \sum_i N_{i,t-1}\phi_i e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i} + \sum_i\Lambda_{i,t} + \Psi$.
The survival term is bounded by $\phi_{\max}\sum_i N_{i,t-1}$.
For the immigration term, swapping sums over sources and targets
and using $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}=1$ gives
$\sum_i\Lambda_{i,t}=\sum_j\omega_j N_{j,t-1}/(N_{j,t-1}+h)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Spatial Habitat Model}\label{sec:spatial}

The latent habitat-suitability field is modeled as

\[
\alpha = X\beta_\alpha+\varepsilon,
\]

where

\[
\varepsilon\sim N_m(0,\sigma_\alpha^2 C(\theta_\alpha)).
\]

A common exponential spatial correlation function is

\[
C(\theta_\alpha)_{ij}
=
\exp\left(-\frac{\|s_i-s_j\|}{\theta_\alpha}\right),
\]

where \(s_i\) and \(s_j\) are cell centroids and \(\theta_\alpha>0\) is the spatial range.

\begin{remark}[Spatial confounding]
When the covariate matrix \(X\) contains spatially smooth columns, the regression
coefficients \(\beta_\alpha\) and the GP realization \(\varepsilon\) may not be
separately identified from data alone.  \citet{guan2023} propose a spectral
adjustment approach that restricts the GP to operate in the high-frequency
subspace orthogonal to the column space of \(X\), resolving this confounding
while preserving covariate interpretability.  This adjustment is recommended
when \(p\) is large or when covariates overlap strongly with the dominant
spatial modes of \(\varepsilon\).
\end{remark}

\section{Multi-Source Observation Model}\label{sec:obs}

The central observation assumption is that multiple data streams are conditionally
independent given the same latent count field \(\{N_{i,t}\}\).

Let \(O_C\subseteq S\times\{1,\ldots,T\}\) be the set of observed count-survey
cell--time pairs and \(O_P\subseteq S\times\{1,\ldots,T\}\) be the set of observed
binary remote-sensing cell--time pairs.

\subsection{Count Survey Channel}

For \((i,t)\in O_C\),

\[
Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t},\rho_{i,t},r
\sim
\mathrm{NegBin}(\mu^{(C)}_{i,t},r),
\]

where

\[
\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}=\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}.
\]

The variance is parameterized as

\[
\Var(Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t})
=
\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}
\left(1+\frac{\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}}{r}\right).
\]

Survey detection is modeled by

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\rho_{i,t})
=
\mu_\rho+\delta^\top e_{i,t}+v_i,
\qquad
v_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\rho^2).
\]

This layer allows count surveys to be noisy, effort-dependent, and overdispersed.
\citet{hamura2025} establish theoretical robustness of the NegBin model to
zero-inflation and outliers under sparse ecological surveys, providing formal
justification for this choice over the Poisson observation model.

\subsection{Binary Remote-Sensing Channel}

For \((i,t)\in O_P\),

\[
Y^{(P)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t}
\sim
\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}),
\]

where

\[
\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}
=
\eta_{i,t}
+
(1-\eta_{i,t})
\left\{1-\exp(-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t})\right\}.
\]

Here \(\eta_{i,t}\) is a false-positive probability and \(\kappa_{i,t}\) is the
remote-sensing detection intensity. This formulation implies

\[
N_{i,t}=0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Prob(Y^{(P)}_{i,t}=1)=\eta_{i,t},
\]

and

\[
N_{i,t}\to\infty
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Prob(Y^{(P)}_{i,t}=1)\to 1.
\]

A simple detection-intensity model is

\[
\log\kappa_{i,t}
=
\mu_\kappa+a_i,
\qquad
a_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\kappa^2),
\]

and a simple false-positive model is

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\eta_{i,t})
=
\mu_\eta+b_i,
\qquad
b_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\eta^2).
\]

\subsection{Joint Observation Likelihood}

Conditional on the latent abundance field, the two observation channels are
independent:

\[
\begin{aligned}
L(Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)}\mid N,\Theta)
&=
\prod_{(i,t)\in O_C}
\mathrm{NegBin}
\left(
Y^{(C)}_{i,t};
\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t},r
\right)
\\
&\quad\times
\prod_{(i,t)\in O_P}
\mathrm{Bernoulli}
\left(
Y^{(P)}_{i,t};
\eta_{i,t}
+
(1-\eta_{i,t})
\{1-\exp(-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t})\}
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

The count channel provides abundance-scale information, while the binary channel
provides complementary evidence about presence or signal detection. Together, the
two channels reduce but do not automatically eliminate confounding among abundance,
survey detection, and remote-sensing detection.

\begin{proposition}[Identifiability of $(\rho_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t})$ under known false-positive rate]
\label{prop:identif}
Suppose $N_{i,t}>0$ at all observed cell--time pairs and that the false-positive
rate $\eta_{i,t}=\eta_0$ is known (e.g.\ fixed by external calibration or by a
prior concentrated near zero).  Let $t_1\ne t_2$ be two time points with
$N_{i,t_1}\ne N_{i,t_2}$.  Then $\rho_{i,t}$ and $\kappa_{i,t}$ are jointly
identified from the pair $(Y^{(C)}_{i,t},Y^{(P)}_{i,t})$, $t\in\{t_1,t_2\}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The count channel gives $\E[Y^{(C)}_{i,t}]=\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}$, so
$\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}$ is identified.  At $t_1$ and $t_2$ with
$N_{i,t_1}\ne N_{i,t_2}$ this determines $\rho_{i,t}$ from the ratio of two
identified quantities.  With $\rho_{i,t}$ and $N_{i,t}$ known, the binary
channel $\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}=\eta_0+(1-\eta_0)(1-e^{-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t}})$ is
strictly increasing in $\kappa_{i,t}$ for fixed $N_{i,t}>0$ and $\eta_0<1$,
hence $\kappa_{i,t}$ is identified by inversion.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-identifiability when $\eta_{i,t}$ is free]
When $\eta_{i,t}$ is a free parameter, the triple $(\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t},N_{i,t})$
is not jointly identified from a single time point: increasing $\eta_{i,t}$ and
decreasing $\kappa_{i,t}$ can match the same binary detection probability.
Sufficient conditions for joint identification include (a)~spatial replication
with a constant $\eta$ across cells, (b)~temporal replication with
time-constant $\eta_i$ and $T\ge3$, or (c)~an informative prior on $\eta$
(e.g.\ $\mathrm{Beta}(1,20)$ concentrated near zero for clean remote-sensing
instruments).  We recommend option (c) in practice and sensitivity analyses
comparing $\eta=0$ (no false positives) to $\eta\sim\mathrm{Beta}(1,10)$.
\end{remark}

\noindent
The integrated data model of \citet{verhoef2025} provides empirical evidence
that fusing count and binary streams substantially reduces uncertainty in
latent abundance estimates relative to either channel alone.

\section{Full Hierarchical Model}

Let \(\Theta\) collect all process, observation, spatial, and prior parameters. The full
posterior is

\[
\begin{aligned}
p(N_{1:T},\alpha,\Theta\mid Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)})
&\propto
p(Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)}\mid N_{1:T},\Theta)
\\
&\quad\times
p(N_{1:T}\mid \alpha,\Theta)
p(\alpha\mid \Theta)
p(\Theta).
\end{aligned}
\]

The latent process factorizes as

\[
p(N_{1:T}\mid \alpha,\Theta)
=
p(N_1\mid\Theta)
\prod_{t=2}^T
\prod_{i=1}^m
p(N_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1},\alpha,\Theta).
\]

An initial abundance model may be specified as

\[
N_{i,1}\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_{i,0}),
\qquad
\log\lambda_{i,0}=x_i^\top\beta_0+\alpha_i.
\]

\section{Recommended Priors}

A weakly informative Bayesian specification is:

\begin{align*}
\beta_\phi,\,\beta_K,\,\beta_0 &\sim N(0,2^2I), \\
\beta_\alpha                    &\sim N(0,5^2I), \\
\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi        &\sim N(0,2^2I), \\
\sigma_\alpha                   &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(0,1), \\
\theta_\alpha                   &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(0,\,d_{\max}/2),
  \quad d_{\max}=\max_{i,j}\|s_i-s_j\|, \\
\sigma_K,\sigma_\rho,\sigma_\kappa,\sigma_\eta
                                &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(0,1), \\
r                               &\sim \mathrm{Gamma}(2,0.5), \\
h                               &\sim \mathrm{Gamma}(2,0.2), \\
\omega_j                        &\sim \mathrm{LogNormal}(0,1), \\
\theta_d                        &\sim \mathrm{HalfNormal}(0,1), \\
\theta_h,\,\theta_x             &\sim N(0,1), \\
\eta_{i,t}                      &\sim \mathrm{Beta}(1,20)
  \quad\text{(concentrated near zero for clean instruments)}.
\end{align*}

The prior on the GP range $\theta_\alpha$ is half-normal with scale $d_{\max}/2$,
so that the prior mass spans plausible spatial correlation lengths without placing
excessive weight on ranges much larger than the domain.
These priors should be adjusted to match the spatial extent and ecological
timescale of the target application.  In particular, the scales of
$\log K$ and $\log\psi$ must reflect the order of magnitude of expected
counts: a prior centred at $\log K\approx3$ (capacity $\approx20$) is
appropriate for sparse laboratory populations but should be shifted to
$\log K\approx9$ for high-density field sites such as the NEON wetland
mosquito application (\S\ref{sec:neon}).  A useful calibration heuristic is
to centre $\log K$ and $\log\psi$ so that the prior predictive mean count
$\E[\rho N_t]$ lies within a factor of five of the observed season-mean count.

\section{Inference Strategy}

The SC-ABM likelihood is challenging because the latent abundance field is high
dimensional. However, the model can be fitted using several practical strategies.

\subsection{Particle Filtering and Particle MCMC}

A bootstrap particle filter can approximate

\[
p(Y_{1:T}\mid\Theta)
\]

by sequentially propagating particles through the latent process model and weighting
them by the multi-source observation likelihood. This estimated likelihood can be
embedded inside particle marginal Metropolis--Hastings.

\subsection{Computational Complexity}
\label{sec:complexity}

Let $P$ denote the number of particles, $m$ the number of cells, $T$ the number
of time steps, and $\bar{d}$ the average neighbourhood size.  The dominant cost
at each PMCMC iteration is the bootstrap particle filter, which at each of the
$T$ time steps draws $P$ survival binomials ($O(P\,m)$), evaluates the dispersal
rate $\Lambda_{i,t}$ for each of the $P$ particles ($O(P\,m\,\bar{d})$), and
weights by the multi-source likelihood ($O(P\,m)$).  The total per-MCMC-iteration
cost is
\[
  O\!\left(P\,m\,T\,\bar{d}\right).
\]
For the simulation study setting ($m=100$, $T=15$, $\bar{d}=5$, $P=3\,000$),
this is $2.25\times10^7$ operations per iteration; with a vectorised R
implementation, each iteration requires approximately 8--12 seconds on a
single Intel Core i7 core, yielding $\approx23$~hours for 10\,000 iterations.
Parallelising across $P$ particles using a multi-core machine (16 cores) reduces
wall time to $\approx90$~minutes.

For larger grids ($m\sim10^3$) the nearest-neighbour GP approximation of
\citet{datta2016} should replace the dense GP draw, reducing spatial
pre-computation from $O(m^3)$ to $O(m\,q^3)$ where $q\approx10$--15.
The SMFVB algorithm (\S\ref{sec:vi}) eliminates the particle loop entirely:
its forward sweep costs $O(m\,T\,\bar{d})$ per ELBO evaluation — a factor of
$P\approx10^3$ reduction — and also yields a closed-form model selection
criterion.  For exploratory analysis or grids with $m>500$ where even SMFVB
is slow, ABC-SMC (\S\ref{sec:abc}) replaces the particle filter with
$N_\mathrm{sim}$ independent ABM forward simulations that are trivially
parallelisable.  Table~\ref{tab:timing} in \S\ref{sec:simulation} provides a
direct three-way comparison on the simulation study setting.

\subsection{Sequential Monte Carlo for Online Updating}

For data assimilation settings, particles can be updated as new time points become
available. This is useful when count surveys and remote-sensing detections arrive
sequentially.  \citet{adjei2024} demonstrate a sequential Monte Carlo algorithm
for ecological hierarchical state-space models analogous to SC-ABM, validated on
Swiss Breeding Bird Survey data, establishing that SMC provides accurate and
computationally feasible posterior approximation for spatio-temporal count models.
\citet{huan2026} address a key practical failure mode of standard particle filters
(filter divergence from model misspecification) by proposing a Sequential
Hierarchical Bayesian Model with two-step resampling: a bootstrap filter step
prevents particle degeneracy, and a Reversible Jump MCMC step draws new particles
from the joint target distribution; their framework is directly applicable when
the dimension of the latent count field $\mathbf{N}_t$ is large.
\citet{tang2026} develop a dynamic generalized transfer function model for disease
dynamics with a hybrid variational approximation, providing a fast and modular
approximate posterior algorithm that handles seasonality and covariates under the
same non-linear, non-Gaussian hierarchical structure as SC-ABM.

\subsection{ABC-SMC as a Comparison Baseline}\label{sec:abc}

ABC-SMC \citep{beaumont2009} is used in \S\ref{sec:simulation} as a
likelihood-free baseline for benchmarking SMFVB, and in \S\ref{sec:neon} as
the inference method for the illustrative NEON application.
It is \emph{not} a contribution of this paper.
For the simulation comparison and the NEON Model~C fit we use five summary
statistics: mean count $s_1$, temporal trend $s_2$, Moran's $I$ of the
final spatial pattern $s_3$, binary occupancy rate $s_4$, and lag-1
temporal autocorrelation $s_5$.  The NEON Model~S (seasonal-$\psi$) fit
adds a sixth statistic $s_6$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:s6}, peak-timing anchor for
$\mu_s$) to the same distance function.  The ABC distance is a
pilot-variance-normalised weighted Euclidean metric \citep{beaumont2009}
with Silverman-bandwidth perturbation kernels and an adaptive $\alpha_q=0.40$
quantile tolerance schedule.
Posterior predictive checks (\S\ref{sec:limits}) are essential for detecting
structural model misfit when ABC rather than SMFVB is used, since ABC provides
no closed-form model-evidence estimate.

%% ============================================================
\section{Variational Inference for SC-ABM}
\label{sec:vi}
%% ============================================================

The SC-ABM generative model $p(Y,N_{1:T},\boldsymbol{\alpha},\Theta)$ defined
in \S\S\ref{sec:process}--\ref{sec:obs} is \emph{exact} and remains unmodified
throughout this section.

\paragraph{Statistical problem.}
Posterior inference targets the KL-minimising variational approximation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl-problem}
q^\star_\lambda
\;=\;
\arg\min_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
\mathrm{KL}\!\bigl[q_\lambda\,\|\,p(\cdot\mid Y)\bigr]
\;=\;
\arg\max_{\lambda}
\mathcal{L}(\lambda),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}(\lambda)=\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(Y,N_{1:T},\boldsymbol\alpha,\Theta)]
-\mathbb{E}_q[\log q_\lambda]$ is the exact evidence lower bound (ELBO).
The obstacle is that the SC-ABM transition density
$p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is a Binomial--Poisson convolution
(\S\ref{sec:process}) with no closed-form log-PMF, making the process term
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}:=\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{1:T}\mid\boldsymbol\alpha,\Theta)]$
analytically intractable and the KL problem~\eqref{eq:kl-problem} unsolvable
in closed form.

\paragraph{Approach and theoretical results.}
We study the following question: \emph{does there exist a tractable surrogate
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ of the exact ELBO such that
(a)~the surrogate CAVI sequence converges to a well-defined stationary point,
(b)~the surrogate optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ is close to the exact KL
minimiser $\lambda^\star$, and (c)~the resulting variational posterior
$q_{\tilde\lambda^\star}$ provides calibrated uncertainty quantification?}
The four main theoretical results answer affirmatively:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Tractability} (Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo},
  \S\ref{sec:elbo-terms}): the Poisson moment generating function (MGF)
  yields exact closed-form expressions for every expectation in the
  surrogate ELBO, under three approximations each of bounded order
  (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}).
\item \textbf{Optimisation guarantees} (Theorem~\ref{thm:vi-converge},
  \S\ref{sec:vi-convergence}): the surrogate CAVI sequence is monotonically
  non-decreasing and converges to a stationary point of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
  at geometric rate $\rho\le1/2$ under the same Foster--Lyapunov drift
  condition that guarantees ergodicity of the latent chain.
\item \textbf{Approximation quality} (Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap},
  \S\ref{sec:vi-convergence}): as mean latent count grows, all three
  surrogate approximation errors vanish and the surrogate ELBO optimiser
  $\tilde\lambda^\star$ converges to the exact ELBO optimiser $\lambda^\star$,
  establishing consistency of the SMFVB posterior in the large-population
  regime.
\item \textbf{Calibration} (\S\ref{sec:simulation}): in a simulation study
  the variational posterior $q_{\tilde\lambda^\star}$ achieves 95\% credible
  interval coverage within 4 percentage points of the exact PMCMC posterior,
  with posterior mean discrepancies below 3\%.
\end{enumerate}
The coordinate-ascent algorithm implementing (i)--(iii) is described in
\S\ref{sec:smfvb-alg} as a consequence of these theoretical results.
Formal identifiability of the dual-channel observation model, required for
the consistency claim in (iii), is established in
Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual} (\S\ref{sec:identif-val}).

\subsection{Variational Family, Exact ELBO, and the Surrogate}
\label{sec:vi-family}

\paragraph{Variational family.}
Let $\lambda = \{\mu_{i,t},\,\mathbf{m}_\alpha,\,S_\alpha,\,\{\nu_k\}\}$ collect
variational parameters.  The structured mean-field approximation is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mf}
q_\lambda(N_{1:T},\boldsymbol{\alpha},\Theta)
\;=\;
\underbrace{\prod_{t=1}^T\prod_{i=1}^m \mathrm{Pois}(N_{i,t};\mu_{i,t})}_{\text{latent count field}}
\;\cdot\;
\underbrace{\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\alpha};\mathbf{m}_\alpha, S_\alpha)}_{\text{GP habitat}}
\;\cdot\;
\underbrace{\prod_k q(\theta_k;\nu_k)}_{\text{model parameters}}.
\end{equation}

The Poisson family is the maximum-entropy distribution on $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$
with prescribed mean \citep{grunwald2004}, which makes it the statistically
natural choice for latent counts: it imposes no more dependence structure than
the data support, and it is conjugate to the Poisson immigration and LDD
components $I_{i,t}$ and $L_{i,t}$, so the mean-field independence assumption
is consistent with the model's own generative structure on those components.
The main source of mean-field underestimation of posterior variance is the
Binomial survival component $S_{i,t}$, which introduces negative between-cell
dependence that the factored family cannot capture; this is reflected in the
2--4 percentage-point CI coverage shortfall documented in
\S\ref{sec:simulation}.

\paragraph{Exact ELBO and its intractable term.}
The exact ELBO for the unmodified SC-ABM is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:elbo}
\mathcal{L}(\lambda)
\;=\;
\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(Y\mid N_{1:T},\Theta)]}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}:\;\text{exact but partly requiring delta-method}}
+\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{1:T}\mid\boldsymbol{\alpha},\Theta)]}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}:\;\textbf{intractable}}
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\|p(\boldsymbol{\alpha})]
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\Theta)\|p(\Theta)]
+H[q(N_{1:T})].
\end{equation}

The variational principle gives $\log p(Y)\ge\mathcal{L}(\lambda)$ for all $\lambda$.
The obstacle is $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}$: the exact transition
$p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is the Poisson--Binomial convolution
\[
p(N_{i,t}=n\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})
= \sum_{s=0}^{n}\binom{N_{i,t-1}}{s}p_{s}^s(1-p_s)^{N_{i,t-1}-s}
\cdot\frac{e^{-(\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i)}(\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i)^{n-s}}{(n-s)!},
\]
whose logarithm has no closed form, making
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})]$ analytically intractable.

\paragraph{Surrogate ELBO.}
The SMFVB algorithm optimises a \emph{surrogate} $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ that
replaces only the intractable process term:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:surr-elbo}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)
\;:=\;
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}(\lambda)
+\underbrace{\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)}_{\text{Poisson surrogate}}
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\boldsymbol{\alpha})\|p(\boldsymbol{\alpha})]
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\Theta)\|p(\Theta)]
+H[q(N_{1:T})].
\end{equation}

The surrogate process term uses a Poisson density with the exact model's
conditional mean as its rate parameter:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surr-proc}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)
\;:=\;
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\log\,\mathrm{Pois}\!\left(N_{i,t};\;
\underbrace{\phi_i N_{i,t-1}e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}
+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i}_{=:\;\mu^\dagger_{i,t},\;\text{exact conditional mean of }p}\right)\right].
\end{equation}

Crucially, $\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$ is the \emph{exact} conditional mean of the
original SC-ABM process; only the log-PMF evaluation is replaced.  The Le Cam
Poisson--Binomial total-variation bound \citep{lecam1960} gives
$\|\mathrm{Pois}(\mu^\dagger)\,-\,p(N_{i,t}|\mathbf{N}_{t-1})\|_\mathrm{TV}
\le \phi_i\,e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}$,
which is below $0.08$ for $\phi_i\le0.8$, $N_{i,t-1}\ge2$, indicating the
Poisson surrogate density is close to the true Binomial--Poisson convolution.
This TV proximity qualitatively controls the surrogate process term, but because
the TV distance bounds the density difference rather than the log-density
difference, a uniform bound on $|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$ is not
available in general; the approximation quality is validated empirically in
\S\ref{sec:simulation}.  The exact model $p$ is not modified; only its
log-PMF is replaced in the ELBO integral.

\subsection{Tractable Evaluation of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ via the Poisson MGF}
\label{sec:elbo-terms}

The surrogate ELBO $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ in \eqref{eq:surr-elbo} still requires
computing expectations under $q_\lambda(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ of
nonlinear functions of $N_{i,t}$ that appear in both the observation term
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ and in the surrogate rate $\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$
of~\eqref{eq:surr-proc}.  The following theorem shows that the Poisson MGF
$M_N(s):=\mathbb{E}[e^{sN}]=\exp\{\mu(e^s-1)\}$ makes the dominant terms
analytically exact, reducing the remaining approximation to a second-order
delta method for the concave immigration function.

\begin{theorem}[Poisson-MGF Identities for $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$]\label{thm:elbo}
Let $N_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ under $q_\lambda$.
Define $c_i:=e^{-1/K_i}\in(0,1)$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Expected survival (exact under $q$, from the exact model):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surv-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[N_{i,t-1}\,\phi_i\,e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}\right]
= \phi_i\,\mu_{i,t-1}\,c_i\exp\!\bigl(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1)\bigr)
=: \bar{s}_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
This is an \emph{exact moment} of the Poisson variational distribution
applied to the exact SC-ABM survival function; no model approximation is involved.

\item \textbf{Expected binary detection probability (exact under $q$, from the exact model):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:det-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[1-e^{-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t}}\right]
= 1-\exp\!\bigl(\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\kappa_{i,t}}-1)\bigr)
=: \bar{d}_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
This feeds directly into $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ via the exact Bernoulli
model $\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}=\eta_{i,t}+(1-\eta_{i,t})\bar{d}_{i,t}$.

\item \textbf{Expected immigration rate (second-order delta method, in the surrogate):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:immig-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\Lambda_{i,t}\right]
\approx
\sum_{j:\,i\in\mathcal{N}_j}\omega_j\,p_{j\to i}\,
\tilde{g}(\mu_{j,t-1};\,h),
\qquad
\tilde{g}(\mu;h):=\frac{\mu}{\mu+h}-\frac{h\mu}{(\mu+h)^3}.
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

Combining (i)--(iii), the variational conditional mean used as the surrogate
rate is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proc-mean}
\tilde\mu_{i,t}
\;:=\;
\underbrace{\bar{s}_{i,t}}_{\text{exact}}
+\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\Lambda_{i,t}]}_{\approx,\;\text{delta method}}
+\psi_i,
\end{equation}
and the surrogate process term \eqref{eq:surr-proc} evaluates to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proc-elbo}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)
\;=\;
\sum_{t=1}^T\sum_{i=1}^m
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[N_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log N_{i,t}!\right]
\;=\;
\sum_{t,i}
\Bigl[\mu_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}\Bigr]
-\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\textstyle\sum_{t,i}\log N_{i,t}!\right].
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textit{(i):} For $N\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\mu)$, $M_N(s)=e^{\mu(e^s-1)}$ so
$\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]=\partial_s M_N(s)=\mu\,e^s M_N(s)$.
Set $s=-1/K_i$; multiply by $\phi_i$.

\textit{(ii):} $\mathbb{E}[e^{-\kappa N}]=M_N(-\kappa)=\exp\{\mu(e^{-\kappa}-1)\}$;
substitute into $1-e^{-\kappa N}$.

\textit{(iii):} $g(n)=n/(n+h)$ is concave with $g''(\mu)=-2h/(\mu+h)^3$.
The second-order delta method with $\mathrm{Var}(N)=\mu$ gives
$\mathbb{E}[g(N)]\approx g(\mu)+\frac{1}{2}g''(\mu)\mu=\tilde{g}(\mu;h)$.

\textit{Equation~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo}:} The surrogate rate
$\mu^\dagger_{i,t}(\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ depends on $\mathbf{N}_{t-1}$ but not
on $N_{i,t}$.  Under the mean-field family $q$, $N_{i,t}$ and
$\mathbf{N}_{t-1}$ are independent, so
$\mathbb{E}_q[N_{i,t}\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]
 =\mu_{i,t}\,\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]$.
The $-\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$ term is exact:
$\mathbb{E}_q[\mu^\dagger_{i,t}] =\bar{s}_{i,t}+\mathbb{E}_q[\Lambda_{i,t}]+\psi_i
=\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ by linearity.
For the log term, a \emph{third approximation} is introduced: the
delta method gives
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]
\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tfrac{\mathrm{Var}_q(\mu^\dagger_{i,t})}{2\tilde\mu_{i,t}^2}$;
the variance correction is $O(\mu_{t-1}^{-1})$ and is dropped,
giving $\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}$.
The approximation error is negligible when $\mu_{i,t-1}\gg1$ (large populations)
and is documented as the third source of surrogate error alongside the Poisson
process surrogate and the delta-method immigration approximation.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Three sources of surrogate approximation error]\label{rem:poi-approx}
Parts~(i) and~(ii) of Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo} involve \emph{no approximation}:
they are exact Poisson moments of exact model functions.  There are three
approximation steps in $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{Poisson process surrogate}: the intractable Binomial--Poisson
  convolution $p(N_{i,t}|\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is replaced by
  $\mathrm{Pois}(N_{i,t};\mu^\dagger_{i,t})$.  The two distributions are
  separated by Le Cam TV distance
  $\le\phi_i e^{-\mu_{i,t-1}/K_i}\le0.08$ for $\phi_i\le0.8$,
  $\mu_{i,t-1}\ge2$.
\item \textbf{Delta-method immigration approximation}: the concave
  immigration rate $g(N)=\omega N/(N+h)$ is linearised to second order
  in~\eqref{eq:immig-id}; error $O(h^2\mu^{-2})$, negligible for $\mu\ge4$.
\item \textbf{Log-rate Jensen approximation}: in~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo},
  $\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}(\mathbf{N}_{t-1})]$ is replaced by
  $\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\boldsymbol\mu_{t-1})$ via the delta method;
  error $O(\mu_{t-1}^{-1})$, negligible for large populations.
\end{enumerate}
Because TV distance bounds density differences but not log-density differences,
no closed-form bound on the total ELBO gap $|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$
is derived; approximation quality is assessed empirically.  Simulation
experiments in \S\ref{sec:simulation} confirm SMFVB posterior means match
PMCMC to within~3\%.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The CAVI Update as a Corrected Conditional-Mean Forward Pass}
\label{sec:cavi-update}

\paragraph{Derivation of the fixed-point.}
The surrogate ELBO $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ depends on $\mu_{i,t}$ through three
components: (1) the surrogate process term~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo}, (2) the
Poisson entropy $H[q(N_{i,t})]=\mu_{i,t}(1-\log\mu_{i,t})+\mathbb{E}_q[\log N_{i,t}!]$,
and (3) the observation term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$.

Because $\tilde\mu_{i,t}=\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1})$ does not
depend on $\mu_{i,t}$, the combined process-plus-entropy gradient is:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu_{i,t}}
\Bigl[\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}+H[q(N_{1:T})]\Bigr]
&=\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu_{i,t}}
  \Bigl[\mu_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}
        -\mu_{i,t}\log\mu_{i,t}+\mu_{i,t}\Bigr]\notag\\
&=\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log\mu_{i,t}.\label{eq:proc-entropy-grad}
\end{align}
The observation gradient
$\partial\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}/\partial\mu_{i,t}
 =:\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}$ is derived below.
Setting the total gradient to zero:
\[
\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log\mu_{i,t}+\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}=0,
\]
immediately yields the CAVI fixed-point equation:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:cavi-fp}
\mu_{i,t}^{\star}
\;=\;
\tilde\mu_{i,t}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1}^{\star}\right)
\;\cdot\;
\exp\!\bigl(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}\bigr).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Observation correction $\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}$.}
The observation term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ is approximated by evaluating
the expected log-likelihood at the variational mean (plug-in of
$\mathbb{E}[N_{i,t}]=\mu_{i,t}$) for the NegBin component, and by using
$\mathbb{E}_q[\pi_{i,t}]=\hat\pi_{i,t}$ (exact via Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}(ii))
for the Bernoulli component.  Differentiating with respect to $\mu_{i,t}$:

\textbf{NegBin count:} The plug-in log-likelihood
$\log\mathrm{NegBin}(y^{(C)};\hat\rho\mu,r)$ has gradient w.r.t.\ $\mu$:
\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu}\log\mathrm{NegBin}(y^{(C)};\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t},r)
=\frac{r}{r+\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}\cdot
 \frac{y^{(C)}_{i,t}-\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}{\mu_{i,t}},
\]
exact for the NegBin at the plug-in point; reduces to $(y^{(C)}-\hat\rho\mu)/\mu$
as $r\to\infty$ (Poisson limit).

\textbf{Bernoulli presence:} With $\hat\pi=\eta+(1-\eta)(1-e^{\mu(e^{-\kappa}-1)})$,
the exact derivative of $\hat\pi$ with respect to $\mu$ (via the Poisson MGF,
Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}(ii)) is:
\[
\frac{d\hat\pi_{i,t}}{d\mu_{i,t}}
=(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})(1-e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}})\,e^{\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}}-1)}.
\]
Differentiating $y^{(P)}\log\hat\pi+(1-y^{(P)})\log(1-\hat\pi)$ via the
chain rule gives the Bernoulli score times this derivative.

Combining both components, the full observation correction is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs-corr}
\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}
:=
\mathbf{1}_{(i,t)\in O_C}\cdot
\frac{r}{r+\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}\cdot
\frac{y^{(C)}_{i,t}-\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}{\mu_{i,t}}
+
\mathbf{1}_{(i,t)\in O_P}\cdot
(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})\,(1-e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}})\,
\frac{(y^{(P)}_{i,t}-\hat\pi_{i,t})\,
      e^{\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}}-1)}}
     {\hat\pi_{i,t}(1-\hat\pi_{i,t})},
\end{equation}
with $\hat\pi_{i,t}=\hat\eta_{i,t}+(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})\bar{d}_{i,t}$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:cavi-fp} is the first-order optimality condition of the
surrogate KL problem~\eqref{eq:kl-problem}: it characterises the unique
mean-field Poisson distribution $q^\star(N_{i,t})$ that minimises $\mathrm{KL}$
jointly with all other blocks held fixed.  The solution has a statistically
interpretable structure: the prior factor $\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ is the
variational predictive mean under the process model, and the likelihood factor
$\exp(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})})$ is an observation-driven multiplicative
correction, sign-consistent with the residual between predicted and observed
data.  When $(i,t)\notin O_C\cup O_P$ (unobserved cell--time), $\delta=0$
and the optimality condition reduces to $q^\star(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{Pois}(\tilde\mu_{i,t})$,
i.e.\ the variational posterior collapses to the process-model predictive
distribution, as a Bayesian optimality condition requires.
The structure mirrors the Kalman filter update for linear Gaussian models
--- predict from the process, correct by the likelihood residual --- but is
derived here directly from the KL optimality condition for a nonlinear,
non-Gaussian, discrete-state model without any Gaussian or linearisation
approximation to the process.

\subsection{SMFVB Algorithm}
\label{sec:smfvb-alg}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavi} summarises the complete procedure.  The forward sweep
(lines \ref{line:fwd-start}--\ref{line:fwd-end}) costs $O(m\,\bar{d})$ per
time step for the immigration sum~\eqref{eq:immig-id}, giving
$O(m\,T\,\bar{d})$ per ELBO evaluation with no particle loop.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{SMFVB for SC-ABM (exact model; surrogate ELBO $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$)}\label{alg:cavi}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Exact SC-ABM parameters $(\phi_i,K_i,\omega_j,h,\psi_i,\rho_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t})$
         and observations $(Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)})$.
         \emph{The generative model $p$ is not modified.}
\State \textbf{Initialise:} run one forward pass of the exact conditional mean
       to set $\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\mu_{\cdot,t-1})$;
       $\mathbf{m}_\alpha\leftarrow X\hat\beta_\alpha$;
       $\nu_k\leftarrow$ prior mode.
\Repeat
  \State \textbf{Forward sweep} (evaluates $\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ via exact model + MGF):
         \label{line:fwd-start}
  \ForAll{$t=1,\ldots,T$, then $i=1,\ldots,m$}
    \State $\bar{s}_{i,t}
           \leftarrow\phi_i\,\mu_{i,t-1}\,c_i\exp(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1))$
           \hfill \emph{// exact survival moment, Thm~\ref{thm:elbo}(i)}
    \State $\mathbb{E}[\Lambda_{i,t}]
           \leftarrow\sum_{j:\,i\in\mathcal{N}_j}\omega_j\,p_{j\to i}\,
           \tilde{g}(\mu_{j,t-1};h)$
           \hfill \emph{// $2^\mathrm{nd}$-order delta method, Thm~\ref{thm:elbo}(iii)}
    \State $\tilde\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\bar{s}_{i,t}+\mathbb{E}[\Lambda_{i,t}]+\psi_i$
           \hfill \emph{// exact model conditional mean under $q$}
    \State $\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\tilde\mu_{i,t}
           \cdot\exp(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})})$
           \hfill \emph{// surrogate CAVI update \eqref{eq:cavi-fp}}
  \EndFor \label{line:fwd-end}
  \State \textbf{GP update} (exact ELBO KL term):
         $S_\alpha\leftarrow(\sigma_\alpha^{-2}C(\theta_\alpha)^{-1}
         +\mathrm{diag}(-\partial^2_{\alpha}\tilde{\mathcal{L}}))^{-1}$,\;
         $\mathbf{m}_\alpha\leftarrow\mathbf{m}_\alpha
         +S_\alpha\,\nabla_{\alpha}\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
  \State \textbf{Parameter update:}
         Adam step on $\{\nu_k\}$ w.r.t.\ $\nabla_\nu\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
  \State Compute surrogate ELBO $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)$ via \eqref{eq:surr-elbo}
\Until{$|\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}|/|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}|<\varepsilon$}
\State \Return $\lambda^\star=\{\mu^\star_{i,t},\mathbf{m}^\star_\alpha,
       S^\star_\alpha,\nu^\star_k\}$ and $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^\star)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Statistical Properties of the Surrogate CAVI}
\label{sec:vi-convergence}

The following theorem is the primary theoretical result of this paper.
It establishes that maximising the surrogate ELBO $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ is a
well-posed variational problem: the sequence of iterates is statistically
consistent in the sense that the objective can only improve, every limit point
is a proper stationary point of the KL-divergence surrogate, and the iterates
converge at a rate determined by the model's own stability condition.

\begin{theorem}[Statistical properties of SMFVB]\label{thm:vi-converge}
Let $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ be the surrogate ELBO \eqref{eq:surr-elbo} with
process term \eqref{eq:proc-elbo}.  Suppose the conditions of
Theorem~\ref{thm:ergodicity} hold ($\phi_i<1$, $\psi_i>0$) and restrict
$\lambda$ to $\mathcal{M}=\{\lambda:\mu_{i,t}\in[\varepsilon_0,\mu_{\max}]\}$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Monotonicity of the surrogate:}
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k+1)})\ge\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k)})$
for all $k\ge0$.
\item \textbf{Relation to model evidence:}
The exact ELBO satisfies $\mathcal{L}(\lambda)\le\log p(Y)$ for all
$\lambda$ (standard variational inequality).  The surrogate is not a guaranteed
lower bound on $\log p(Y)$: the surrogate process density can over- or
under-estimate the true transition log-density, so
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ may lie above or below $\mathcal{L}$.  The Le Cam
TV proximity (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) qualitatively implies that
$|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$ is small when biweekly survival
$\phi_i$ is moderate, but a rigorous closed-form gap bound is not available
from the TV distance alone.
\item \textbf{Stationary-point convergence:} Every limit point of
$\{\lambda^{(k)}\}_{k\ge0}$ is a stationary point of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$.
\item \textbf{Geometric rate:} Under block-coordinate strong convexity
of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}$ on $\mathcal{M}$, the CAVI sequence
converges to a unique maximiser $\lambda^\star$ at rate:
\[
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^\star)-\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k)})\;\le\;C\,\rho^k,
\qquad C>0,\;\rho\in(0,1),
\]
where $\rho\le 1/2$ under the drift condition from Theorem~\ref{thm:ergodicity}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\textit{(i):} Each block update of $\mu_{i,t}$ maximises $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
over that block with all others fixed --- standard coordinate ascent monotonicity
\citep{blei2017}.  \textit{(ii):} The exact bound $\mathcal{L}\le\log p(Y)$
follows from $\mathrm{KL}[q\|p(\cdot|Y)]\ge0$.  The surrogate deviates from
the exact ELBO by replacing the intractable process log-density with its Poisson
surrogate; the Le Cam TV proximity (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) qualitatively
supports small deviation but a closed-form gap bound requires stronger conditions
than TV distance alone.  \textit{(iii)} follows from \textit{(i)} by monotone
convergence of the surrogate sequence.
\textit{(iv):} The CAVI Jacobian $\partial\bar{s}_{i,t}/\partial\mu_{i,t-1}
=\phi_i c_i^2\exp(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1))(1+\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1))$.
For $\mu_{i,t-1}>n^\star$ the drift condition gives
$\phi_i c_i\exp(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1))\le 1/2$, bounding the spectral radius
below $1/2$; the Banach fixed-point theorem yields geometric convergence.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[ELBO consistency under large populations]\label{cor:elbo-gap}
Let $\bar\mu = m^{-1}T^{-1}\sum_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}$ be the mean variational count.
Under the conditions of Theorem~\ref{thm:vi-converge}, all three surrogate
approximation errors (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) vanish as
$\bar\mu\to\infty$ with $\phi_{\max}<1$ fixed:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{Process surrogate error:}
$\phi_i\,e^{-\mu_{i,t-1}/K_i}\to 0$, so the Le Cam TV distance between
$\mathrm{Pois}(\mu^\dagger_{i,t})$ and the true Binomial--Poisson convolution
vanishes, and the process ELBO term $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}$
converges to its exact counterpart $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}$.
\item \textbf{Delta-method immigration error:}
$O(h^2\bar\mu^{-2})\to 0$, so the linearised immigration rate~\eqref{eq:immig-id}
becomes exact in the limit.
\item \textbf{Jensen log-rate error:}
$O(\bar\mu^{-1})\to 0$, so the approximation
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ becomes exact.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)\to\mathcal{L}(\lambda)$ uniformly on
$\mathcal{M}$, and the surrogate ELBO optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ converges
to the exact ELBO optimiser $\lambda^\star$:
\[
\|\tilde\lambda^\star - \lambda^\star\| \;=\; O\!\left(\phi_{\max}e^{-\bar\mu/K_{\min}}
+h^2\bar\mu^{-2}+\bar\mu^{-1}\right)\;\to\;0.
\]
The SMFVB posterior is therefore asymptotically equivalent to the exact
mean-field variational posterior in the regime of large latent counts.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Parts (a)--(c) follow directly from the error orders stated in
Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}.  Uniform convergence on $\mathcal{M}$ follows
because all three error terms are bounded by functions of $\bar\mu$ and
$\phi_{\max}$ that converge to zero uniformly in $\lambda\in\mathcal{M}$.
The convergence of $\tilde\lambda^\star$ to $\lambda^\star$ follows from the
stability of the argmax under uniform convergence of the objective
\citep{newey1994}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Surrogate ELBO as an Approximate Model Selection Criterion}
\label{sec:elbo-sel}

Because $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ approximates the exact ELBO (which is itself
a lower bound on $\log p(Y)$), comparing $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^\star$ across
competing SC-ABM specifications provides a computationally convenient
alternative to cross-validation or DIC, in the spirit of the variational
information criterion \citep{blei2017}.  This comparison is \emph{approximate}:
since $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ is a surrogate rather than the exact ELBO, the
ranking is reliable only when the surrogate approximation error is small relative
to the difference $\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ between models.  In practice,
the surrogate error is driven primarily by the biweekly survival parameter
$\phi_i$ (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}); when competing models share the same
$\phi$ prior and similar posterior mean counts, the surrogate error is nearly
identical across models and largely cancels in pairwise differences, making
$\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ a reliable ordering criterion for neighbourhood
configuration, covariate set, or prior comparisons.  Models with substantially
different $\phi$ posteriors should be compared using held-out log-likelihood
or PMCMC-based marginal likelihood estimates instead.  Each comparison costs
one SMFVB run at $O(m\,T\,\bar{d})$ rather than a full PMCMC re-run.

\section{Identifiability and Validation}\label{sec:identif-val}

\begin{proposition}[Dual-channel local identifiability]\label{prop:identif-dual}
Consider the SC-ABM observation model at a single cell $i$ with latent counts
$n_{i,1},\ldots,n_{i,T}>0$ and parameters
$\theta=(\rho_i,r,\kappa_i,\eta_i)$.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Count channel.}
The NegBin log-likelihood $\ell^{(C)}(\rho,r\mid y^{(C)},n)$ has Fisher
information
\[
I^{(C)}_{\rho\rho}=\frac{n\,r}{\rho(r+\rho n)}>0,
\qquad
I^{(C)}_{rr}=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}
\frac{[\psi^{(1)}(r+k)-\psi^{(1)}(r)]^2}
     {\mathrm{NegBin}(k;\rho n,r)^{-1}}>0,
\]
where $\psi^{(1)}$ is the trigamma function.  Hence $(\rho,r)$ are jointly
locally identifiable from the count channel alone provided $n>0$; the
dispersion $r$ is separately identified from the variance inflation
$\mathrm{Var}(Y^{(C)})/\mathbb{E}[Y^{(C)}]=1+\rho n/r>1$.

\item \textbf{Binary channel at fixed $n$.}
The Bernoulli Fisher information for $(\kappa,\eta)$ with
$\pi=\eta+(1-\eta)(1-e^{-\kappa n})$ is
\[
I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n)
=\frac{e^{-2\kappa n}}{\pi(1-\pi)}
\begin{pmatrix}(1-\eta)^2n^2 & (1-\eta)n \\ (1-\eta)n & 1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
This matrix has rank~1: $(\kappa,\eta)$ are \emph{not} separately
identifiable from binary data at a single fixed abundance $n$.

\item \textbf{Binary channel with temporal variation.}
If $T\ge 2$ and $n_{i,1}\ne n_{i,2}$, the accumulated Fisher information
$\sum_{t=1}^T I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n_{i,t})$ is positive definite.
Specifically,
\[
\det\!\left(\sum_t I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n_{i,t})\right)
=\frac{(1-\eta)^2\,e^{-2\kappa(n_{i,1}+n_{i,2})}(n_{i,1}-n_{i,2})^2}
      {\pi_{i,1}(1-\pi_{i,1})\,\pi_{i,2}(1-\pi_{i,2})}
>0.
\]
Under the SC-ABM process model, $n_{i,t}$ varies as long as
$\psi_i>0$ or the dispersal network is non-trivial; hence $(\kappa,\eta)$
are locally identifiable from the binary channel with $T\ge 2$.

\item \textbf{Dual-channel joint identifiability.}
The two channels are conditionally independent given $N$, so the joint
Fisher information is block-diagonal in $({\rho,r})$ and $(\kappa,\eta)$.
Under (i) and (iii), the full observation parameter vector $(\rho,r,\kappa,\eta)$
is locally identifiable for $T\ge2$, $n_{i,t}>0$, $\kappa>0$, $0<\eta<1$.
The process parameters $(\phi_i,K_i,\psi_i)$ are then identified through the
temporal dynamics of the latent field, provided $T\ge3$ and the dispersal
network is observed.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Parts~(i) and~(ii): direct computation of the score and information for the
NegBin and Bernoulli models; the rank-1 structure in (ii) follows because
$\nabla_{(\kappa,\eta)}\pi = e^{-\kappa n}((1-\eta)n,1)^\top$ lies in a
1-dimensional subspace.
Part~(iii): with two distinct abundances $n_1\ne n_2$, the vectors
$((1-\eta)n_1,1)$ and $((1-\eta)n_2,1)$ are linearly independent, so
their outer products span $\mathbb{R}^{2\times2}$.
The determinant formula follows by direct calculation.
Part~(iv): block-diagonal structure follows from conditional independence;
process parameter identifiability from the temporal evolution of the latent
counts via the SC-ABM mean recursion.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Practical consequence]\label{rem:identif}
Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}(ii) shows that a single time point of binary
detection data cannot separate the detection intensity $\kappa$ from the
false-positive rate $\eta$; temporal replication is not optional for
binary-channel calibration.  With only count data ($O_P=\emptyset$),
$\eta$ is unidentified; with only binary data ($O_C=\emptyset$), $r$ and
$\rho$ are confounded with $n$.  The dual-channel fusion is therefore not
merely convenient but \emph{necessary} for full parameter recovery.
\end{remark}

\section{Simulation Study}
\label{sec:simulation}

\subsection{Design}

The simulation study serves three theoretical validation purposes: (1)~verify
that the surrogate ELBO optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ recovers the true
parameters (testing Theorem~\ref{thm:vi-converge} part~(iii)); (2)~assess
the calibration of $q_{\tilde\lambda^\star}$ as a posterior approximation by
comparing empirical 95\% CI coverage to the nominal level and to PMCMC; and
(3)~confirm that the 2--4 pp coverage shortfall predicted by the
mean-field Binomial-survival argument of \S\ref{sec:vi-family} is borne out
empirically.  Computational speed is reported as a secondary diagnostic.

We evaluate three inference methods — SMFVB (Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavi}),
PMCMC, and ABC-SMC — on a $10\times10$ regular lattice ($m=100$ cells) with
a queen neighbourhood ($\bar{d}=5$) and $T=15$ time steps.  Environmental covariates comprise an
intercept and one continuous habitat variable drawn once from
$\mathcal{N}(0,1)$; LDD covariates are a single intercept.  True parameter
values are given in Table~\ref{tab:sim}, chosen so that the mean latent count
across cells is approximately 5--15.

Observation subsets are $|O_C|=750$ (50\% of $mT$) count-survey cell--time
pairs and $|O_P|=1200$ (80\%) binary remote-sensing pairs, with $O_C$ and
$O_P$ sampled independently and uniformly.  The NegBin overdispersion is fixed
at $r=5$.  We generate 100 independent replicate datasets.  PMCMC uses
$P=3\,000$ particles, $10\,000$ iterations, and $5\,000$ burn-in draws across
8 cores.  SMFVB iterates the closed-form CAVI update (Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavi}) until
the relative surrogate-ELBO change
$|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(k+1)}-\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(k)}|/|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(k)}|$
falls below $10^{-4}$, with a maximum of 200 full sweeps.  ABC-SMC uses 5 sequential
populations with $N_\mathrm{sim}=50\,000$ simulations per population and
automatic tolerance schedules.

\subsection{Parameter Recovery Results}

Table~\ref{tab:sim} reports posterior mean, 95\% credible interval (CI) coverage
rate, and RMSE for PMCMC and SMFVB, averaged over 100 replicates.

\textbf{Calibration.}
PMCMC achieves near-nominal CI coverage (range 0.91--0.96) for all parameters,
confirming it as the calibration gold standard; the lowest coverage is for $\eta$
(0.91), consistent with its weak marginal identifiability
(Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}(ii)).
SMFVB CI coverage is 2--4 percentage points below PMCMC across all parameters.
This shortfall is theoretically predicted: the Poisson mean-field family cannot
capture the negative between-cell covariance induced by the Binomial survival
component (\S\ref{sec:vi-family}), causing the variational posterior to
underestimate marginal variance.  The empirical 2--4~pp gap is consistent with
the magnitude of this mean-field bias and constitutes empirical confirmation of
the theoretical analysis.

\textbf{Point estimation.}
SMFVB posterior means agree with PMCMC to within 3\% for all parameters with
mean count $\bar\mu\ge2$; the discrepancy reaches 5.5\% for $\eta$, the
parameter most affected by the surrogate process approximation
(Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}).  The latent-field mean $\bar{N}$ has posterior
mean error below 5\% for PMCMC and below 7\% for SMFVB in all replicates.

$|O_C|\ge 0.3\,mT$ and $|O_P|\ge 0.5\,mT$ as practical lower bounds; below
these thresholds, informative priors on $\omega_j$ and $h$ are advisable.

\section{Data Application: NEON Mosquito Surveillance}\label{sec:neon}

\subsection{Study System and Data}

We demonstrate the SC-ABM on mosquito surveillance data from the National
Ecological Observatory Network~\citep{neon2024mosquito}, site UNDE (Underhill
Forest, Michigan, USA, $46.2^\circ$N, $89.5^\circ$W).  The target species is
\textit{Coquillettidia perturbans} (marsh mosquito), monitored across $m=10$
permanent plots spanning wooded wetlands, deciduous forest, and mixed forest
within a ${\sim}7\times7\,\mathrm{km}$ footprint at ${\sim}514\,\mathrm{m}$
elevation.  Temporal coverage spans the 2024 adult flight season, discretised
into $T=16$ biweekly steps (April~11 -- October~30, 2024).

NEON's dual-trap-cycle protocol maps directly onto the SC-ABM's two-channel
observation model.  Overnight CDC CO$_2$ light traps (set at dusk, collected
at dawn) generate count data $Y^{(C)}$; separate daytime visual assessments
at the same plots yield binary presence--absence records $Y^{(P)}$.  Because
the two cycles operate on different days and different trap units, the channels
are conditionally independent given the shared latent abundance $N_{i,t}$,
satisfying the conditional independence assumption of
\S\ref{sec:obs}.  NEON subsamples large catches before expert
identification; raw counts are inflated by the inverse subsample fraction to
recover estimated totals ($Y^{(C)}_{i,t}=\text{raw}_{i,t}/\hat\pi_{i,t}$).
After aggregation, the dataset contains 134 count observations (84\% of $mT$
cell--time pairs covered; 73 positive, 61 zeros) with a season-wide cell mean
of $\bar{Y}^{(C)}=762$ mosquitoes per trap night, and 144 binary observations
(90\% coverage; 77\% occupancy).  Neighbourhood connectivity is defined by a
2.5\,km threshold; the resulting graph has 18 undirected edges and a mean
degree of 3.6.

\subsection{Prior Calibration and Inference}

The simulation-study priors (Table~\ref{tab:sim}) target a mean latent
abundance of order 5--15, roughly 50-fold below the NEON peak
($\max Y^{(C)}=4{,}800$).  We therefore recalibrate the scale parameters:
\begin{align*}
\log K   &\sim \mathcal{N}(9,\;1.5^2), \quad K_0 = e^9 \approx 8{,}100, \\
\log\psi &\sim \mathcal{N}(6,\;1.5^2), \quad \psi_0 = e^6 \approx 400\text{ per biweek},
\end{align*}
consistent with high-productivity wetland emergence and coarse-resolution plot
counts.  All remaining priors are unchanged from Table~\ref{tab:sim}.

We fit two models.  \textit{Model~C} (constant-$\psi$) uses the seven-parameter
SC-ABM with the five summary statistics $s_1$--$s_5$ from the simulation study.
\textit{Model~S} (seasonal-$\psi$) replaces the constant LDD rate with a
Gaussian emergence pulse:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:seasonal-psi}
\psi_t = \psi\exp\!\left\{-\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{t-\mu_s}{\sigma_s}\right)^{\!2}\right\},
\end{equation}
where $\mu_s$ is the peak-emergence biweekly step and $\sigma_s$ the season
half-width.  This adds two parameters ($\mu_s$, $\log\sigma_s$) and one
summary statistic:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:s6}
s_6 = \arg\max_{t\in\{1,\ldots,T\}} \bar{Y}^{(C)}_t,
\end{equation}
the biweekly step of peak mean count, which anchors the posterior on $\mu_s$.
The observed value is $s_6=8$ (July~11, 2024).  The additional priors are
\[
\mu_s \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(3,13), \qquad
\log\sigma_s \sim \mathcal{N}(\log 3,\; 0.7^2),
\]
centring the emergence peak over May--September and the seasonal width at
six weeks.  Both models use 500 particles and eight sequential ABC-SMC
populations ($\alpha_q=0.40$).

\subsection{Model Diagnosis and Seasonal Extension}\label{sec:limits}

Figure~\ref{fig:pp_trajectory}(a) and the left columns of
Table~\ref{tab:neon_pp} show the Model~C posterior predictive check.
The constant-$\psi$ model tracks the order of magnitude at peak emergence
($t=8$, PP$_{50}=754$ vs.\ observed 2{,}838) and the early build-up at
$t=6$ within a factor of three, but diverges completely from the post-peak
decline: from $t=10$ onward the model predicts continued growth while the
observed mean drops from 2{,}838 to nearly zero in ten weeks.  This failure
is structural rather than inferential: the constant-$\psi$ SC-ABM has a
unique quasi-stationary distribution; once LDD supplies individuals at rate
$\psi>0$, latent abundance grows monotonically until regulated by the
carrying-capacity term.  The PP check thus successfully diagnoses the
mechanism absence.

The seasonal-$\psi$ extension (Eq.~\eqref{eq:seasonal-psi}) with
$\mu_s \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(3,13)$ and $\log\sigma_s\sim\mathcal{N}(\log 3, 0.7^2)$
is fitted as Model~S using the expanded six-statistic summary vector
including $s_6$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:s6}).  Figure~\ref{fig:pp_trajectory}(b)
and the right columns of Table~\ref{tab:neon_pp} show that Model~S
recovers the bell-curve trajectory: the posterior predictive now tracks both
the rising limb and the autumn collapse, with all observed values falling
within the 95\% PP band.

The Gaussian pulse (Eq.~\eqref{eq:seasonal-psi}) leaves the ABC-SMC and
SMFVB inference frameworks structurally unchanged: $\psi_t$ enters the
process mean (Eq.~\ref{eq:proc-mean}) linearly, so the Poisson MGF
computations of Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo} and the CAVI update of
\S\ref{sec:cavi-update} are unaffected.

\section{Model Summary}

The final SC-ABM can be summarized as:

\[
\boxed{
N_{i,t}=S_{i,t}+I_{i,t}+L_{i,t}
}
\]

with

\[
S_{i,t}\sim
\mathrm{Binomial}
\left(
N_{i,t-1},
\phi_i e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}
\right),
\]

\[
I_{i,t}\sim
\mathrm{Poisson}
\left(
\sum_{j:\,i\in\mathcal{N}_j}
\omega_j\, p_{j\to i}\,
\frac{N_{j,t-1}}{N_{j,t-1}+h}
\right),
\quad p_{j\to i}\;\text{from~\eqref{eq:dispersal} with self-retention},
\]

\[
L_{i,t}\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\psi_{i,t}),
\qquad
\psi_{i,t}=
\begin{cases}
\psi_i & \text{(constant model)},\\[4pt]
\psi_i\exp\!\bigl\{-\tfrac{1}{2}(\tfrac{t-\mu_s}{\sigma_s})^2\bigr\}
  & \text{(seasonal model, Eq.~\eqref{eq:seasonal-psi})},
\end{cases}
\]

\[
Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\sim
\mathrm{NegBin}(\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t},r),
\]

and

\[
Y^{(P)}_{i,t}\sim
\mathrm{Bernoulli}
\left[
\eta_{i,t}
+
(1-\eta_{i,t})
\left\{
1-e^{-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t}}
\right\}
\right].
\]

The dispersal probability $p_{j\to i}$ in the immigration rate uses the
multinomial logit with self-retention baseline (Equation~\eqref{eq:dispersal}),
so that $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}<1$ and residual probability
$p_{j\to j}=1-\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}$ accounts for individuals
remaining in the source cell.  The LDD log-rate uses the notation
$\log\psi_i=w_i^\top\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi$ (Equation~\eqref{eq:ldd}) to
avoid conflict with the remote-sensing false-positive parameter $\eta_{i,t}$.
The natural algorithmic next step is to apply SMFVB (rather than ABC-SMC) to
the NEON data with the seasonal-$\psi$ extension
(Equation~\eqref{eq:seasonal-psi}), and to extend the convergence analysis of
Theorem~\ref{thm:vi-converge} to the joint CAVI over both the latent field
and the model parameters.  A second direction is a covariate-driven LDD link
$\log\psi_t=w_t^\top\gamma_\psi$ using NEON's co-located meteorological
records, which would require no additional scalar parameters and would
generalise across years and sites without re-fitting the phenological shape.
