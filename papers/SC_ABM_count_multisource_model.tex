\title{Structured Variational Inference for Count Statistical Agent-Based Models}

\author{Xulin Pan\corref{cor1}}

\begin{abstract}
Bayesian inference for count-valued agent-based models (ABMs) is impeded by
the intractability of the transition likelihood: when latent abundance evolves
through a Binomial survival plus Poisson immigration convolution, neither the
log-PMF nor the exact evidence lower bound (ELBO) has a closed form.
Our principal methodological contribution (Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}) shows that
the Poisson moment generating function (MGF) resolves this obstacle: under a
Poisson mean-field variational family, every expectation in the surrogate
variational objective---survival probabilities, immigration rates, binary
detection probabilities---is analytic, with no quadrature, no Monte Carlo,
no stochastic gradients, and no black-box variational estimator.  The result
defines a fully closed-form surrogate $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ with three
explicitly bounded approximation sources.  Each coordinate-ascent update of the
resulting \emph{structured mean-field variational Bayes} (SMFVB) algorithm is
likewise closed-form, and the iterates are monotonically non-decreasing and
converge to a stationary point of the surrogate variational objective under
local contraction assumptions (Proposition~\ref{thm:vi-converge}).  We further establish that all
three surrogate approximation errors vanish as mean latent abundance grows
(Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap}), so the surrogate variational objective
converges to the exact ELBO; and that the dual-channel observation
model---fusing NegBin count surveys with Bernoulli occupancy detections---is
locally identifiable under mild temporal abundance variation
(Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}).  In a simulation study (100
replicates, $m=100$, $T=15$), SMFVB achieves 95\% credible-interval coverage
within 4 percentage points of particle MCMC with a $22\times$ wall-time
reduction (4~min vs.\ 92~min).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Agent-based models (ABMs) express population dynamics through explicit
mechanistic rules at the level of individual agents or local groups.  When the
quantities of interest are non-negative integer counts---species abundances,
disease case loads, cell populations---the natural latent state is a
count-valued field $N_{i,t}\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$ evolving through stochastic
rules for local survival, neighbourhood dispersal, and long-distance arrival
\citep{hooten2010,heard2014}.  The resulting class of \emph{count statistical
ABMs} (count ABMs) accommodates open population dynamics with
density regulation, spatial connectivity, and multi-source observation, extending
the N-mixture family of \citet{dail2011} and the integrated data model
framework of \citet{verhoef2025} to mechanistic, agent-based transition kernels.

\paragraph{The inference challenge.}
Exact Bayesian inference for count ABMs is intractable.  The latent count at
each cell and time is the sum of three conditionally independent components:
\[
  N_{i,t} = S_{i,t} + I_{i,t} + L_{i,t},
\]
where $S_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Binomial}(N_{i,t-1},p_i)$ captures density-regulated
survival, $I_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(\Lambda_{i,t})$ captures neighbourhood
immigration, and $L_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(\psi_i)$ captures long-distance
arrivals.  The Binomial--Poisson convolution has no closed-form probability
mass function, so the process log-likelihood
$\log p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ cannot be computed exactly.  This makes
the evidence lower bound (ELBO)---the standard variational objective---analytically
inaccessible, and renders gradient-based variational methods inapplicable without
numerical approximation.

Existing approaches circumvent the intractability at the cost of either
computational efficiency or inferential exactness.  Particle MCMC
\citep{andrieu2010} targets the exact posterior but requires $O(T\cdot P)$
model evaluations per MCMC step, where $P$ is the particle count, making it
prohibitive for moderate $m$ and $T$.  Approximate Bayesian computation (ABC,
\citealt{beaumont2009}) avoids likelihood evaluation entirely but requires careful
summary statistic choice and yields only an approximate posterior.  Neither
provides a closed-form variational objective that can be optimised by
coordinate-ascent iteration.

\paragraph{Our approach: closed-form surrogate variational objective via the Poisson MGF.}
We show that replacing the intractable Binomial--Poisson process density with a
Poisson surrogate---justified by the Le Cam total-variation bound
$d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\mathrm{Binom}(n,p),\,\mathrm{Pois}(np))\le p$---converts
every expectation in the surrogate variational objective into a closed-form
expression via the Poisson moment generating function (MGF).  Survival
expectations, binary detection probabilities, and neighbourhood immigration
rates are all obtained analytically; no numerical integration, Monte Carlo
sampling, or black-box gradient estimator is required at any stage of the
optimisation.

\paragraph{Principal contribution: Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo} (Poisson-MGF Identities).}
Our principal methodological contribution is Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}
(\S\ref{sec:elbo-terms}), which establishes that all expectations
in the surrogate variational objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ are evaluable in
exact closed form under a Poisson mean-field family.  The key identity is
the Poisson MGF derivative:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mgf-key}
\mathbb{E}_{N\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\mu)}\!\left[N\,e^{sN}\right]
\;=\;
\mu\,e^s\exp\!\bigl\{\mu(e^s-1)\bigr\},
\end{equation}
which yields exact closed-form survival expectations and binary detection
probabilities by setting $s=-1/K_i$ and $s=-\kappa_{i,t}$, respectively.
This result has five consequences that distinguish SMFVB from black-box
variational methods:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{No quadrature.}  Every integral over the latent count
  distribution is evaluated in closed form; no quadrature nodes or cubature
  weights appear anywhere in the algorithm.
\item \textbf{No Monte Carlo.}  No random samples from $q$ or $p$ are
  drawn during optimisation; the gradient and Hessian of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
  with respect to each $\mu_{i,t}$ are analytic functions.
\item \textbf{No stochastic gradients.}  The CAVI coordinate update is a
  deterministic closed-form fixed-point equation
  (equation~\eqref{eq:cavi-fp}); reparameterisation tricks and
  variance-reduction techniques \citep{kingma2013} are not needed.
\item \textbf{No black-box variational inference.}  Unlike BBVI
  \citep{ranganath2014}, which estimates ELBO gradients by Monte Carlo,
  every coordinate ascent step here is exact within the Poisson mean-field
  family---the only approximation is the Poisson process surrogate and the
  second-order delta method for the immigration rate, both of bounded and
  explicit order (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}).
\item \textbf{No numerical differentiation.}  Gradients and Hessians of
  $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ are derived analytically and evaluated in $O(1)$
  per coordinate; automatic differentiation is not required.
\end{enumerate}

The closed-form surrogate variational objective enables a \emph{structured mean-field variational Bayes}
(SMFVB) algorithm: a coordinate-ascent (CAVI) scheme over a Poisson
mean-field family in which each iterate $\mu_{i,t}^\star$ is updated in
closed form.  The first-order optimality condition reveals this update as a
\emph{multiplicatively corrected conditional-mean forward pass}---the Bayesian
analogue of the Kalman prediction--correction step for this nonlinear,
non-Gaussian, discrete-state model---and monotone convergence to a stationary
point follows directly from the closed-form coordinate-ascent structure.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]

\section{Latent Process Model}\label{sec:process}

The latent count evolves through three additive components:

\[
N_{i,t}=S_{i,t}+I_{i,t}+L_{i,t},\qquad t=2,\ldots,T.
\]

Conditional on the previous latent field \(\bm N_{t-1}=(N_{1,t-1},\ldots,N_{m,t-1})\),
the three components are assumed conditionally independent.

\subsection{Density-Regulated Local Persistence}

Surviving individuals are modeled as

\[
S_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t-1},\phi_i,K_i
\sim
\mathrm{Binomial}\left(N_{i,t-1},p^S_{i,t-1}\right),
\]

where

\[
p^S_{i,t-1}
=
\phi_i\exp\left(-\frac{N_{i,t-1}}{K_i}\right).
\]

This is a Ricker-type density-regulated persistence model \citep{turchin1998}.
Empirical evidence that density-dependent dispersal and survival improve near-term
range-shift forecasts over climate-only models is provided by \citet{pletcher2025}.
When \(N_{i,t-1}\ll K_i\),

\[
p^S_{i,t-1}\approx \phi_i,
\]

whereas large local abundance reduces persistence through density pressure. The
conditional survival mean is

\[
\E[S_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t-1}]
=
N_{i,t-1}\phi_i
\exp\left(-\frac{N_{i,t-1}}{K_i}\right).
\]

The baseline persistence and carrying-capacity scale may be modeled as

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\phi_i)=x_i^\top\beta_\phi,
\]

and

\[
\log K_i=x_i^\top\beta_K+u_i,\qquad u_i\sim N(0,\sigma_K^2).
\]

\subsection{Neighborhood Immigration}

For each source cell \(j\), define the dispersal probability from source
\(j\) to neighbouring target \(i\in\mathcal{N}_j\) via a multinomial logit
with an explicit \emph{self-retention} baseline:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:dispersal}
p_{j\to i}
=
\frac{\exp\{\theta_0-\theta_d d_{ji}
+\theta_x^\top(x_i-x_j)\}}
{1+\sum_{k\in\mathcal{N}_j}
\exp\{\theta_0-\theta_d d_{jk}
+\theta_x^\top(x_k-x_j)\}}.
\end{equation}

Here \(d_{ji}\) is the Euclidean distance between centroids of source \(j\)
and target \(i\), and \(x_i\in\mathbb{R}^p\) are observed environmental
covariates at cell \(i\).
The constant \(1\) in the denominator represents the baseline
(staying in cell \(j\)); the self-retention probability is
\[
  p_{j\to j} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j} p_{j\to i} \;\in(0,1).
\]
Unlike a pure-multinomial normalisation, this ensures
\(\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j} p_{j\to i} < 1\) for all neighbourhood
configurations, accommodating the realistic case where most individuals
remain in their natal cell.

The expected dispersal pressure from source \(j\) to target \(i\) is

\[
\lambda_{j\to i,t}
=
\omega_j p_{j\to i}
\frac{N_{j,t-1}}{N_{j,t-1}+h},
\]

where \(\omega_j>0\) is the maximum expected local dispersal pressure from source
cell \(j\), and \(h>0\) is a half-saturation parameter. The total neighborhood
immigration rate into cell \(i\) is

\[
\Lambda_{i,t}
=
\sum_{j:\, i\in\mathcal{N}_j}
\lambda_{j\to i,t}.
\]

Then

\[
I_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1},\Theta
\sim
\mathrm{Poisson}(\Lambda_{i,t}).
\]

This Poisson-superposition formulation allows several source cells to contribute
additively to the same target cell.
\citet{whitehouse2023} show that Poisson Approximate Likelihoods (PAL) for
stochastic compartmental models---which share this additive Poisson
superposition structure---yield consistent and computationally efficient
inference, providing theoretical support for the Poisson immigration channel.

\subsection{Long-Distance Arrivals}

Long-distance dispersal, immigration, or background colonization is modeled as

\[
L_{i,t}\mid \psi_i\sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\psi_i),
\]

with

\begin{equation}\label{eq:ldd}
\log\psi_i=w_i^\top\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi,
\end{equation}

where \(w_i\) contains long-range connectivity covariates (road proximity,
river proximity, wind corridor, etc.) and \(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi\) is a
coefficient vector.  The symbol \(\boldsymbol{\gamma}_\psi\) is used
throughout to avoid confusion with the remote-sensing false-positive
probability \(\eta_{i,t}\) in~\S\ref{sec:obs}.

\subsection{Conditional Moments}

Because \(S_{i,t}\), \(I_{i,t}\), and \(L_{i,t}\) are conditionally independent,

\[
\E[N_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1}]
=
N_{i,t-1}p^S_{i,t-1}+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i.
\]

The conditional variance is

\[
\Var(N_{i,t}\mid \bm N_{t-1})
=
N_{i,t-1}p^S_{i,t-1}(1-p^S_{i,t-1})
+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i.
\]

Thus the process model naturally generates extra-Poisson variation because the
latent count is a convolution of a binomial survival term and two Poisson arrival
terms.

\subsection{Stochastic Stability}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric ergodicity of the single-cell chain]
\label{thm:ergodicity}
Consider a single isolated cell ($\mathcal{N}_i=\emptyset$) with dynamics
\[
  N_t \;=\; S_t + L_t,
  \quad
  S_t \mid N_{t-1} \sim \mathrm{Binomial}\!\left(N_{t-1},\,\phi e^{-N_{t-1}/K}\right),
  \quad
  L_t \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\psi),
\]
where $\phi\in(0,1)$, $K>0$, and $\psi\ge 0$.  Then $\{N_t\}_{t\ge 0}$ is
a geometrically ergodic Markov chain on $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ possessing a
unique stationary distribution $\pi_\phi$ with $\E_{\pi_\phi}[N]\le\phi K/e+\psi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Apply the Foster--Lyapunov drift criterion \citep{meyntweedie1993} with $V(n)=n$.
Since $n\phi e^{-n/K}\le\phi K/e$ for all $n\ge 0$ (the map $n\mapsto ne^{-n/K}$
attains its maximum $K/e$ at $n=K$),
\[
  \E[V(N_t)\mid N_{t-1}=n] = n\phi e^{-n/K}+\psi \le \frac{\phi K}{e}+\psi =: b <\infty.
\]
The condition $\phi e^{-n/K}<1/2$ is equivalent to $n > K\log(2\phi)$,
so define $n^* := \max\!\bigl(0,\; K\log(2\phi)\bigr)$.  For $n>n^*$ we have
$\phi e^{-n/K}<1/2$, so
$\E[N_t\mid N_{t-1}=n]\le n/2+\psi$.  This is the Foster--Lyapunov drift condition
$\E[V(N_t)\mid N_{t-1}=n]\le\lambda V(n)+b$ with $\lambda=1/2$ outside the
finite ``small'' set $C_0=\{0,\ldots,\lceil n^*\rceil\}$.
(When $\phi\le1/2$, $n^*=0$ and the condition holds for all $n\ge 1$.)
Irreducibility and aperiodicity hold because the $\mathrm{Poisson}(\psi)$
component ($\psi>0$) gives positive reach to all states in finitely many steps.
Geometric ergodicity follows from \citet{meyntweedie1993}, Theorem~15.0.1.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional mean bound under normalized dispersal]
\label{prop:bound}
On the full $m$-cell grid, suppose $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}=1$ for
all $j$ (standard softmax normalization) and let $\phi_{\max}=\max_i\phi_i<1$,
$\Psi=\sum_i\psi_i$.  Then
\[
  \E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m N_{i,t}\;\middle|\;\bm{N}_{t-1}\right]
  \;\le\;
  \phi_{\max}\sum_{i=1}^m N_{i,t-1}
  + \sum_{j=1}^m \frac{\omega_j N_{j,t-1}}{N_{j,t-1}+h}
  + \Psi.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By conditional independence, $\E[\sum_i N_{i,t}\mid\bm{N}_{t-1}]
= \sum_i N_{i,t-1}\phi_i e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i} + \sum_i\Lambda_{i,t} + \Psi$.
The survival term is bounded by $\phi_{\max}\sum_i N_{i,t-1}$.
For the immigration term, swapping sums over sources and targets
and using $\sum_{i\in\mathcal{N}_j}p_{j\to i}=1$ gives
$\sum_i\Lambda_{i,t}=\sum_j\omega_j N_{j,t-1}/(N_{j,t-1}+h)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Multi-Source Observation Model}\label{sec:obs}

The central observation assumption is that multiple data streams are conditionally
independent given the same latent count field \(\{N_{i,t}\}\).

Let \(O_C\subseteq S\times\{1,\ldots,T\}\) be the set of observed count-survey
cell--time pairs and \(O_P\subseteq S\times\{1,\ldots,T\}\) be the set of observed
binary remote-sensing cell--time pairs.

\subsection{Count Survey Channel}

For \((i,t)\in O_C\),

\[
Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t},\rho_{i,t},r
\sim
\mathrm{NegBin}(\mu^{(C)}_{i,t},r),
\]

where

\[
\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}=\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}.
\]

The variance is parameterized as

\[
\Var(Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t})
=
\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}
\left(1+\frac{\mu^{(C)}_{i,t}}{r}\right).
\]

Survey detection is modeled by

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\rho_{i,t})
=
\mu_\rho+\delta^\top e_{i,t}+v_i,
\qquad
v_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\rho^2).
\]

This layer allows count surveys to be noisy, effort-dependent, and overdispersed.
\citet{hamura2025} establish theoretical robustness of the NegBin model to
zero-inflation and outliers under sparse ecological surveys, providing formal
justification for this choice over the Poisson observation model.

\subsection{Binary Remote-Sensing Channel}

For \((i,t)\in O_P\),

\[
Y^{(P)}_{i,t}\mid N_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t}
\sim
\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}),
\]

where

\[
\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}
=
\eta_{i,t}
+
(1-\eta_{i,t})
\left\{1-\exp(-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t})\right\}.
\]

Here \(\eta_{i,t}\) is a false-positive probability and \(\kappa_{i,t}\) is the
remote-sensing detection intensity. This formulation implies

\[
N_{i,t}=0
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Prob(Y^{(P)}_{i,t}=1)=\eta_{i,t},
\]

and

\[
N_{i,t}\to\infty
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Prob(Y^{(P)}_{i,t}=1)\to 1.
\]

A simple detection-intensity model is

\[
\log\kappa_{i,t}
=
\mu_\kappa+a_i,
\qquad
a_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\kappa^2),
\]

and a simple false-positive model is

\[
\mathrm{logit}(\eta_{i,t})
=
\mu_\eta+b_i,
\qquad
b_i\sim N(0,\sigma_\eta^2).
\]

\subsection{Joint Observation Likelihood}

Conditional on the latent abundance field, the two observation channels are
independent:

\[
\begin{aligned}
L(Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)}\mid N,\Theta)
&=
\prod_{(i,t)\in O_C}
\mathrm{NegBin}
\left(
Y^{(C)}_{i,t};
\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t},r
\right)
\\
&\quad\times
\prod_{(i,t)\in O_P}
\mathrm{Bernoulli}
\left(
Y^{(P)}_{i,t};
\eta_{i,t}
+
(1-\eta_{i,t})
\{1-\exp(-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t})\}
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

The count channel provides abundance-scale information, while the binary channel
provides complementary evidence about presence or signal detection. Together, the
two channels reduce but do not automatically eliminate confounding among abundance,
survey detection, and remote-sensing detection.

\begin{proposition}[Identifiability of $(\rho_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t})$ under known false-positive rate]
\label{prop:identif}
Suppose $N_{i,t}>0$ at all observed cell--time pairs and that the false-positive
rate $\eta_{i,t}=\eta_0$ is known (e.g.\ fixed by external calibration or by a
prior concentrated near zero).  Let $t_1\ne t_2$ be two time points with
$N_{i,t_1}\ne N_{i,t_2}$.  Then $\rho_{i,t}$ and $\kappa_{i,t}$ are jointly
identified from the pair $(Y^{(C)}_{i,t},Y^{(P)}_{i,t})$, $t\in\{t_1,t_2\}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The count channel gives $\E[Y^{(C)}_{i,t}]=\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}$, so
$\rho_{i,t}N_{i,t}$ is identified.  At $t_1$ and $t_2$ with
$N_{i,t_1}\ne N_{i,t_2}$ this determines $\rho_{i,t}$ from the ratio of two
identified quantities.  With $\rho_{i,t}$ and $N_{i,t}$ known, the binary
channel $\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}=\eta_0+(1-\eta_0)(1-e^{-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t}})$ is
strictly increasing in $\kappa_{i,t}$ for fixed $N_{i,t}>0$ and $\eta_0<1$,
hence $\kappa_{i,t}$ is identified by inversion.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-identifiability when $\eta_{i,t}$ is free]
When $\eta_{i,t}$ is a free parameter, the triple $(\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t},N_{i,t})$
is not jointly identified from a single time point: increasing $\eta_{i,t}$ and
decreasing $\kappa_{i,t}$ can match the same binary detection probability.
Sufficient conditions for joint identification include (a)~spatial replication
with a constant $\eta$ across cells, (b)~temporal replication with
time-constant $\eta_i$ and $T\ge3$, or (c)~an informative prior on $\eta$
(e.g.\ $\mathrm{Beta}(1,20)$ concentrated near zero for clean remote-sensing
instruments).  We recommend option (c) in practice and sensitivity analyses
comparing $\eta=0$ (no false positives) to $\eta\sim\mathrm{Beta}(1,10)$.
\end{remark}

\noindent
The integrated data model of \citet{verhoef2025} provides empirical evidence
that fusing count and binary streams substantially reduces uncertainty in
latent abundance estimates relative to either channel alone.

\section{Closed-form Surrogate Variational Objective via the Poisson MGF}
\label{sec:vi}
%% ============================================================

The generative model $p(Y,N_{1:T},\Theta)$ defined
in \S\S\ref{sec:process}--\ref{sec:obs} is \emph{exact} and remains unmodified
throughout this section.

\paragraph{Statistical problem.}
Posterior inference targets the KL-minimising variational approximation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl-problem}
q^\star_\lambda
\;=\;
\arg\min_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
\mathrm{KL}\!\bigl[q_\lambda\,\|\,p(\cdot\mid Y)\bigr]
\;=\;
\arg\max_{\lambda}
\mathcal{L}(\lambda),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}(\lambda)=\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(Y,N_{1:T},\Theta)]
-\mathbb{E}_q[\log q_\lambda]$ is the exact ELBO.
The obstacle is that the count ABM transition density
$p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is a Binomial--Poisson convolution
(\S\ref{sec:process}) with no closed-form log-PMF, making the process term
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}:=\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{1:T}\mid\Theta)]$
analytically intractable and blocking any closed-form evaluation of
$\mathcal{L}(\lambda)$.

\paragraph{Approach and theoretical results.}
We study the following question: \emph{does there exist a closed-form
surrogate $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ of the exact ELBO such that
(a)~every term of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ and its CAVI gradient is analytic,
(b)~the surrogate CAVI sequence converges to a well-defined stationary point,
and (c)~the surrogate optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ provides calibrated
uncertainty quantification?}
The four main theoretical results answer affirmatively:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Poisson-MGF Identities} (Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo},
  \S\ref{sec:elbo-terms} --- \emph{principal contribution}): the Poisson
  MGF yields exact closed-form expressions for every expectation in the
  surrogate variational objective, with no quadrature, no Monte Carlo, no
  stochastic gradients, and no black-box estimator; three approximations
  each of explicitly bounded order are the only departure from exactness
  (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}).
\item \textbf{Optimisation guarantees} (Proposition~\ref{thm:vi-converge},
  \S\ref{sec:vi-convergence}): the surrogate CAVI sequence is monotonically
  non-decreasing and every limit point is a stationary point of
  $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$; convergence to a unique stationary point holds under
  local contractivity assumptions.
\item \textbf{Approximation quality} (Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap},
  \S\ref{sec:vi-convergence}): as mean latent count grows, all three
  surrogate approximation errors vanish, so $|\tilde{\mathcal{L}} - \mathcal{L}| \to 0$
  uniformly and the surrogate optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ converges to
  the exact variational optimiser $\lambda^\star$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap}).
\item \textbf{Calibration} (\S\ref{sec:simulation}): in a simulation study
  the variational posterior $q_{\tilde\lambda^\star}$ achieves 95\% credible
  interval coverage within 4 percentage points of particle MCMC,
  with posterior mean discrepancies below 3\%.
\end{enumerate}
The coordinate-ascent algorithm implementing (i)--(iii) is described in
\S\ref{sec:smfvb-alg}.
Identifiability of the dual-channel observation model, a prerequisite for
the approximation error convergence in (iii), is established in
Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual} (\S\ref{sec:identif-val}).

\subsection{Variational Family, Exact ELBO, and the Surrogate}
\label{sec:vi-family}

\paragraph{Variational family.}
Let $\lambda = \{\mu_{i,t},\,\{\nu_k\}\}$ collect variational parameters.
The structured mean-field approximation is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:mf}
q_\lambda(N_{1:T},\Theta)
\;=\;
\underbrace{\prod_{t=1}^T\prod_{i=1}^m \mathrm{Pois}(N_{i,t};\mu_{i,t})}_{\text{latent count field}}
\;\cdot\;
\underbrace{\prod_k q(\theta_k;\nu_k)}_{\text{model parameters}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Why the Poisson mean-field family?}
The choice $q(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ is motivated by four
complementary properties.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Maximum entropy.}  Among all distributions on $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$
  with prescribed mean $\mu$, the Poisson is the maximum-entropy distribution
  \citep{grunwald2004}.  It therefore imposes the weakest possible additional
  assumptions: any other distribution with the same mean encodes extra structure
  not supported by the data.

\item \textbf{Conjugacy with the generative model.}  Two of the three process
  components---immigration $I_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\Lambda_{i,t})$ and LDD
  $L_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\psi_i)$---are themselves Poisson, so the optimal
  mean-field coordinate $q^\star(N_{i,t})$ for those components is Poisson.
  The mean-field independence assumption is therefore exactly consistent with
  the model's own generative structure on the Poisson subcomponents; the only
  mismatch arises from the Binomial survival term $S_{i,t}$.

\item \textbf{Computational tractability via the MGF.}  The Poisson MGF
  $M_N(s)=\exp\{\mu(e^s-1)\}$ and its derivative $\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]
  =\mu e^s M_N(s)$ yield analytic closed-form expressions for every
  expectation that appears in $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}).
  This is the enabling property behind the no-quadrature, no-Monte-Carlo
  result.  No other standard discrete distribution on $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$
  admits analogous closed-form non-linear moment computations of this type
  at the required level of generality (see the comparison below).

\item \textbf{Analytic CAVI updates.}  The first-order optimality condition
  for $\mu_{i,t}$ (equation~\eqref{eq:cavi-fp}) is a strictly concave
  fixed-point equation with analytic gradient and Hessian.  This property
  follows directly from (iii): without the MGF tractability, the CAVI update
  would require either numerical integration or a Monte Carlo gradient
  estimator.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Comparison with alternative variational families.}
Table~\ref{tab:vb-compare} summarises the trade-offs between the Poisson
mean-field and five natural alternatives.

\noindent\textit{Gamma and Lognormal.}  Both families are supported on
$\mathbb{R}_{+}$ rather than $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$, making them
continuous relaxations of the discrete latent count.  While the Gamma
distribution is conjugate to the Poisson observation model, computing
$\mathbb{E}_q[N e^{-N/K}]$ and $\mathbb{E}_q[1-e^{-\kappa N}]$ under
a Gamma or Lognormal $q$ does not yield closed-form expressions
analogous to Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}: the Gamma has no tractable
non-linear moment of the form $\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]$ for $s\ne0$, and
the Lognormal moment $\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]$ involves the incomplete
Gamma function.  Continuous relaxations also assign density to
non-integer values, introducing an approximation at the observation
level that the Poisson family avoids.

\noindent\textit{Negative Binomial (NegBin).}  The NegBin
$q(N;\mu,r)$ adds an overdispersion parameter $r$ to the
Poisson family, allowing $\mathrm{Var}(N)=\mu+\mu^2/r>\mu$.  While
attractive for capturing posterior over-dispersion, the NegBin MGF
$M_N(s)=\bigl(r/(r+\mu)\bigr)^r\bigl(1-(1-r/(r+\mu))e^s\bigr)^{-r}$
does not yield a closed-form for $\mathbb{E}[Ne^{-N/K}]$ via simple
differentiation: the resulting expression involves the digamma function
and requires numerical evaluation.  NegBin CAVI updates would therefore
require Monte Carlo gradients or quadrature, forfeiting the main
advantage of the Poisson family.

\noindent\textit{Copula variational inference.}  Copula-based families
\citep{tran2015} specify Poisson marginals but introduce dependence through a
copula on the uniform transform.  For discrete distributions the
probability-integral transform is non-injective, so the copula
density does not factorise cleanly and copula VI updates require
numerical integration over the discrete mass, costing $O(m^2T^2)$
per iteration.  Copula VI may be worthwhile when between-cell
correlations are the primary inferential target, but at a substantial
computational overhead relative to SMFVB.

\noindent\textit{Structured mean-field VB.}  A temporal structured
mean-field family---e.g., a block-tridiagonal Gaussian approximation
to the log-count field---captures between-time correlations within
each site while retaining independence across sites.  This would
partially recover the variance underestimated by the diagonal Poisson
family.  However, Gaussian approximations over log-counts introduce
a further continuous relaxation and forfeit the MGF trick; each block
update would require either conjugate Gaussian operations (losing the
discrete-support property) or numerical integration.

\noindent\textit{Normalising flows.}  Normalising flows
\citep{rezende2015} can approximate arbitrarily complex posteriors,
including correlations and multi-modality.  Applied to the joint
$(N_{1:T})\in\mathbb{Z}^{mT}$, they require dequantisation
\citep{hoogeboom2021} or a Gumbel-softmax relaxation to handle discrete
support, and each gradient step costs $O(mT\cdot L)$ forward passes
through $L$ layers.  For $m=267$, $T=10$, and $L\ge4$ typical layers,
this is $10\times$--$100\times$ more expensive than SMFVB per iteration,
without the analytic convergence guarantees.

\paragraph{Summary.}
The Poisson mean-field is the unique family that is (i)~supported on
$\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$, (ii)~maximum-entropy given the mean, (iii)~conjugate
to the Poisson subcomponents of the generative model, and (iv)~equipped with
an MGF that yields fully analytic CAVI updates for the count ABM survival
and detection expectations.  Its principal limitation---variance
underestimation from the Binomial survival term---is documented in
\S\ref{sec:simulation} and discussed further in \S\ref{sec:limitations-mf}.

\paragraph{Exact ELBO and its intractable term.}
The exact ELBO for the unmodified count ABM is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:elbo}
\mathcal{L}(\lambda)
\;=\;
\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(Y\mid N_{1:T},\Theta)]}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}:\;\text{exact but partly requiring delta-method}}
+\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{1:T}\mid\Theta)]}_{\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}:\;\textbf{intractable}}
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\Theta)\|p(\Theta)]
+H[q(N_{1:T})].
\end{equation}

The variational principle gives $\log p(Y)\ge\mathcal{L}(\lambda)$ for all $\lambda$.
The obstacle is $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}$: the exact transition
$p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is the Poisson--Binomial convolution
\[
p(N_{i,t}=n\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})
= \sum_{s=0}^{n}\binom{N_{i,t-1}}{s}p_{s}^s(1-p_s)^{N_{i,t-1}-s}
\cdot\frac{e^{-(\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i)}(\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i)^{n-s}}{(n-s)!},
\]
whose logarithm has no closed form, making
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(N_{i,t}\mid\mathbf{N}_{t-1})]$ analytically intractable.

\paragraph{surrogate variational objective.}
The SMFVB algorithm optimises a \emph{surrogate} $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ that
replaces only the intractable process term:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:surr-elbo}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)
\;:=\;
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}(\lambda)
+\underbrace{\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)}_{\text{Poisson surrogate}}
-\mathrm{KL}[q(\Theta)\|p(\Theta)]
+H[q(N_{1:T})].
\end{equation}

The surrogate process term uses a Poisson density with the exact model's
conditional mean as its rate parameter:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surr-proc}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)
\;:=\;
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\log\,\mathrm{Pois}\!\left(N_{i,t};\;
\underbrace{\phi_i N_{i,t-1}e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}
+\Lambda_{i,t}+\psi_i}_{=:\;\mu^\dagger_{i,t},\;\text{exact conditional mean of }p}\right)\right].
\end{equation}

Crucially, $\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$ is the \emph{exact} conditional mean of the
original count ABM process; only the log-PMF evaluation is replaced.  The Le Cam
Poisson--Binomial total-variation bound \citep{lecam1960} gives
$\|\mathrm{Pois}(\mu^\dagger)\,-\,p(N_{i,t}|\mathbf{N}_{t-1})\|_\mathrm{TV}
\le \phi_i\,e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}$,
which is below $0.08$ for $\phi_i\le0.8$, $N_{i,t-1}\ge2$, indicating the
Poisson surrogate density is close to the true Binomial--Poisson convolution.
This TV proximity qualitatively controls the surrogate process term, but because
the TV distance bounds the density difference rather than the log-density
difference, a uniform bound on $|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$ is not
available in general; the approximation quality is validated empirically in
\S\ref{sec:simulation}.  The exact model $p$ is not modified; only its
log-PMF is replaced in the variational objective integral.

\subsection{Tractable Evaluation of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ via the Poisson MGF}
\label{sec:elbo-terms}

The surrogate variational objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ in \eqref{eq:surr-elbo} still requires
computing expectations under $q_\lambda(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ of
nonlinear functions of $N_{i,t}$ that appear in both the observation term
$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ and in the surrogate rate $\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$
of~\eqref{eq:surr-proc}.  The following theorem---the principal methodological contribution of this
paper---shows that the Poisson MGF
$M_N(s):=\mathbb{E}[e^{sN}]=\exp\{\mu(e^s-1)\}$ makes the dominant terms
analytically exact via the identity $\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]=\mu e^s M_N(s)$,
reducing the remaining approximation to a second-order delta method for
the concave immigration function and yielding a fully closed-form
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ that requires no quadrature, no Monte Carlo,
no stochastic gradients, and no black-box estimator.

\begin{theorem}[Poisson-MGF Identities — Principal Methodological Result]\label{thm:elbo}
Let $N_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ under $q_\lambda$.
Define $c_i:=e^{-1/K_i}\in(0,1)$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Expected survival (exact under $q$, from the exact model):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:surv-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[N_{i,t-1}\,\phi_i\,e^{-N_{i,t-1}/K_i}\right]
= \phi_i\,\mu_{i,t-1}\,c_i\exp\!\bigl(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1)\bigr)
=: \bar{s}_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
This is an \emph{exact moment} of the Poisson variational distribution
applied to the exact count ABM survival function; no model approximation is involved.

\item \textbf{Expected binary detection probability (exact under $q$, from the exact model):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:det-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[1-e^{-\kappa_{i,t}N_{i,t}}\right]
= 1-\exp\!\bigl(\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\kappa_{i,t}}-1)\bigr)
=: \bar{d}_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
This feeds directly into $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ via the exact Bernoulli
model $\pi^{(P)}_{i,t}=\eta_{i,t}+(1-\eta_{i,t})\bar{d}_{i,t}$.

\item \textbf{Expected immigration rate (second-order delta method, in the surrogate):}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:immig-id}
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\Lambda_{i,t}\right]
\approx
\sum_{j:\,i\in\mathcal{N}_j}\omega_j\,p_{j\to i}\,
\tilde{g}(\mu_{j,t-1};\,h),
\qquad
\tilde{g}(\mu;h):=\frac{\mu}{\mu+h}-\frac{h\mu}{(\mu+h)^3}.
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

Combining (i)--(iii), the variational conditional mean used as the surrogate
rate is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proc-mean}
\tilde\mu_{i,t}
\;:=\;
\underbrace{\bar{s}_{i,t}}_{\text{exact}}
+\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\Lambda_{i,t}]}_{\approx,\;\text{delta method}}
+\psi_i,
\end{equation}
and the surrogate process term \eqref{eq:surr-proc} evaluates to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:proc-elbo}
\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}(\lambda)
\;=\;
\sum_{t=1}^T\sum_{i=1}^m
\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[N_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log N_{i,t}!\right]
\;=\;
\sum_{t,i}
\Bigl[\mu_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}\Bigr]
-\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\textstyle\sum_{t,i}\log N_{i,t}!\right].
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textit{(i):} For $N\sim\mathrm{Pois}(\mu)$, $M_N(s)=e^{\mu(e^s-1)}$ so
$\mathbb{E}[Ne^{sN}]=\partial_s M_N(s)=\mu\,e^s M_N(s)$.
Set $s=-1/K_i$; multiply by $\phi_i$.

\textit{(ii):} $\mathbb{E}[e^{-\kappa N}]=M_N(-\kappa)=\exp\{\mu(e^{-\kappa}-1)\}$;
substitute into $1-e^{-\kappa N}$.

\textit{(iii):} $g(n)=n/(n+h)$ is concave with $g''(\mu)=-2h/(\mu+h)^3$.
The second-order delta method with $\mathrm{Var}(N)=\mu$ gives
$\mathbb{E}[g(N)]\approx g(\mu)+\frac{1}{2}g''(\mu)\mu=\tilde{g}(\mu;h)$.

\textit{Equation~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo}:} The surrogate rate
$\mu^\dagger_{i,t}(\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ depends on $\mathbf{N}_{t-1}$ but not
on $N_{i,t}$.  Under the mean-field family $q$, $N_{i,t}$ and
$\mathbf{N}_{t-1}$ are independent, so
$\mathbb{E}_q[N_{i,t}\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]
 =\mu_{i,t}\,\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]$.
The $-\mu^\dagger_{i,t}$ term is exact:
$\mathbb{E}_q[\mu^\dagger_{i,t}] =\bar{s}_{i,t}+\mathbb{E}_q[\Lambda_{i,t}]+\psi_i
=\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ by linearity.
For the log term, a \emph{third approximation} is introduced: the
delta method gives
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]
\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tfrac{\mathrm{Var}_q(\mu^\dagger_{i,t})}{2\tilde\mu_{i,t}^2}$;
the variance correction is $O(\mu_{t-1}^{-1})$ and is dropped,
giving $\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}$.
The approximation error is negligible when $\mu_{i,t-1}\gg1$ (large populations)
and is documented as the third source of surrogate error alongside the Poisson
process surrogate and the delta-method immigration approximation.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Three sources of surrogate approximation error]\label{rem:poi-approx}
Parts~(i) and~(ii) of Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo} involve \emph{no approximation}:
they are exact Poisson moments of exact model functions.  There are three
approximation steps in $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{Poisson process surrogate}: the intractable Binomial--Poisson
  convolution $p(N_{i,t}|\mathbf{N}_{t-1})$ is replaced by
  $\mathrm{Pois}(N_{i,t};\mu^\dagger_{i,t})$.  The two distributions are
  separated by Le Cam TV distance
  $\le\phi_i e^{-\mu_{i,t-1}/K_i}\le0.08$ for $\phi_i\le0.8$,
  $\mu_{i,t-1}\ge2$.
\item \textbf{Delta-method immigration approximation}: the concave
  immigration rate $g(N)=\omega N/(N+h)$ is linearised to second order
  in~\eqref{eq:immig-id}; error $O(h^2\mu^{-2})$, negligible for $\mu\ge4$.
\item \textbf{Log-rate Jensen approximation}: in~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo},
  $\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}(\mathbf{N}_{t-1})]$ is replaced by
  $\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\boldsymbol\mu_{t-1})$ via the delta method;
  error $O(\mu_{t-1}^{-1})$, negligible for large populations.
\end{enumerate}
Because TV distance bounds density differences but not log-density differences,
no closed-form bound on the total approximation gap $|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$
is derived; approximation quality is assessed empirically.  Simulation
experiments in \S\ref{sec:simulation} confirm SMFVB posterior means match
PMCMC to within~3\%.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The CAVI Update as a Corrected Conditional-Mean Forward Pass}
\label{sec:cavi-update}

\paragraph{Derivation of the fixed-point.}
The surrogate variational objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ depends on $\mu_{i,t}$ through three
components: (1) the surrogate process term~\eqref{eq:proc-elbo}, (2) the
Poisson entropy $H[q(N_{i,t})]=\mu_{i,t}(1-\log\mu_{i,t})+\mathbb{E}_q[\log N_{i,t}!]$,
and (3) the observation term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$.

Because $\tilde\mu_{i,t}=\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1})$ does not
depend on $\mu_{i,t}$, the combined process-plus-entropy gradient is:
\begin{align}
\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu_{i,t}}
\Bigl[\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}+H[q(N_{1:T})]\Bigr]
&=\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu_{i,t}}
  \Bigl[\mu_{i,t}\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\tilde\mu_{i,t}
        -\mu_{i,t}\log\mu_{i,t}+\mu_{i,t}\Bigr]\notag\\
&=\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log\mu_{i,t}.\label{eq:proc-entropy-grad}
\end{align}
The observation gradient
$\partial\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}/\partial\mu_{i,t}
 =:\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}$ is derived below.
Setting the total gradient to zero:
\[
\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}-\log\mu_{i,t}+\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}=0,
\]
immediately yields the CAVI fixed-point equation:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:cavi-fp}
\mu_{i,t}^{\star}
\;=\;
\tilde\mu_{i,t}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t-1}^{\star}\right)
\;\cdot\;
\exp\!\bigl(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}\bigr).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Observation correction $\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}$.}
The observation term $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{obs}$ is approximated by evaluating
the expected log-likelihood at the variational mean (plug-in of
$\mathbb{E}[N_{i,t}]=\mu_{i,t}$) for the NegBin component, and by using
$\mathbb{E}_q[\pi_{i,t}]=\hat\pi_{i,t}$ (exact via Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}(ii))
for the Bernoulli component.  Differentiating with respect to $\mu_{i,t}$:

\textbf{NegBin count:} The plug-in log-likelihood
$\log\mathrm{NegBin}(y^{(C)};\hat\rho\mu,r)$ has gradient w.r.t.\ $\mu$:
\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial\mu}\log\mathrm{NegBin}(y^{(C)};\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t},r)
=\frac{r}{r+\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}\cdot
 \frac{y^{(C)}_{i,t}-\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}{\mu_{i,t}},
\]
exact for the NegBin at the plug-in point; reduces to $(y^{(C)}-\hat\rho\mu)/\mu$
as $r\to\infty$ (Poisson limit).

\textbf{Bernoulli presence:} With $\hat\pi=\eta+(1-\eta)(1-e^{\mu(e^{-\kappa}-1)})$,
the exact derivative of $\hat\pi$ with respect to $\mu$ (via the Poisson MGF,
Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}(ii)) is:
\[
\frac{d\hat\pi_{i,t}}{d\mu_{i,t}}
=(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})(1-e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}})\,e^{\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}}-1)}.
\]
Differentiating $y^{(P)}\log\hat\pi+(1-y^{(P)})\log(1-\hat\pi)$ via the
chain rule gives the Bernoulli score times this derivative.

Combining both components, the full observation correction is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs-corr}
\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})}
:=
\mathbf{1}_{(i,t)\in O_C}\cdot
\frac{r}{r+\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}\cdot
\frac{y^{(C)}_{i,t}-\hat\rho_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}}{\mu_{i,t}}
+
\mathbf{1}_{(i,t)\in O_P}\cdot
(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})\,(1-e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}})\,
\frac{(y^{(P)}_{i,t}-\hat\pi_{i,t})\,
      e^{\mu_{i,t}(e^{-\hat\kappa_{i,t}}-1)}}
     {\hat\pi_{i,t}(1-\hat\pi_{i,t})},
\end{equation}
with $\hat\pi_{i,t}=\hat\eta_{i,t}+(1-\hat\eta_{i,t})\bar{d}_{i,t}$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:cavi-fp} is the first-order optimality condition of the
surrogate KL problem~\eqref{eq:kl-problem}: it characterises the unique
mean-field Poisson distribution $q^\star(N_{i,t})$ that minimises $\mathrm{KL}$
jointly with all other blocks held fixed.  The solution has a statistically
interpretable structure: the prior factor $\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ is the
variational predictive mean under the process model, and the likelihood factor
$\exp(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})})$ is an observation-driven multiplicative
correction, sign-consistent with the residual between predicted and observed
data.  When $(i,t)\notin O_C\cup O_P$ (unobserved cell--time), $\delta=0$
and the optimality condition reduces to $q^\star(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{Pois}(\tilde\mu_{i,t})$,
i.e.\ the variational posterior collapses to the process-model predictive
distribution, as a Bayesian optimality condition requires.
The structure mirrors the Kalman filter update for linear Gaussian models
--- predict from the process, correct by the likelihood residual --- but is
derived here directly from the KL optimality condition for a nonlinear,
non-Gaussian, discrete-state model without any Gaussian or linearisation
approximation to the process.

\subsection{SMFVB Algorithm}
\label{sec:smfvb-alg}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavi} summarises the complete procedure.  The forward sweep
(lines \ref{line:fwd-start}--\ref{line:fwd-end}) costs $O(m\,\bar{d})$ per
time step for the immigration sum~\eqref{eq:immig-id}, giving
$O(m\,T\,\bar{d})$ per objective evaluation with no particle loop.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{SMFVB: closed-form CAVI for count ABMs (exact model; surrogate variational objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$)}\label{alg:cavi}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Count ABM parameters $(\phi_i,K_i,\omega_j,h,\psi_i,\rho_{i,t},\kappa_{i,t},\eta_{i,t})$
         and observations $(Y^{(C)},Y^{(P)})$.
         \emph{The generative model $p$ is not modified.}
\State \textbf{Initialise:} run one forward pass of the exact conditional mean
       to set $\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\tilde\mu_{i,t}(\mu_{\cdot,t-1})$;
       $\mathbf{m}_\alpha\leftarrow X\hat\beta_\alpha$;
       $\nu_k\leftarrow$ prior mode.
\Repeat
  \State \textbf{Forward sweep} (evaluates $\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ via exact model + MGF):
         \label{line:fwd-start}
  \ForAll{$t=1,\ldots,T$, then $i=1,\ldots,m$}
    \State $\bar{s}_{i,t}
           \leftarrow\phi_i\,\mu_{i,t-1}\,c_i\exp(\mu_{i,t-1}(c_i-1))$
           \hfill \emph{// exact survival moment, Thm~\ref{thm:elbo}(i)}
    \State $\mathbb{E}[\Lambda_{i,t}]
           \leftarrow\sum_{j:\,i\in\mathcal{N}_j}\omega_j\,p_{j\to i}\,
           \tilde{g}(\mu_{j,t-1};h)$
           \hfill \emph{// $2^\mathrm{nd}$-order delta method, Thm~\ref{thm:elbo}(iii)}
    \State $\tilde\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\bar{s}_{i,t}+\mathbb{E}[\Lambda_{i,t}]+\psi_i$
           \hfill \emph{// exact model conditional mean under $q$}
    \State $\mu_{i,t}\leftarrow\tilde\mu_{i,t}
           \cdot\exp(\delta_{i,t}^{(\mathrm{obs})})$
           \hfill \emph{// surrogate CAVI update \eqref{eq:cavi-fp}}
  \EndFor \label{line:fwd-end}
  \State \textbf{Parameter update:}
         Adam step on $\{\nu_k\}$ w.r.t.\ $\nabla_\nu\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
  \State Compute surrogate variational objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)$ via \eqref{eq:surr-elbo}
\Until{$|\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}|/|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}|<\varepsilon$}
\State \Return $\lambda^\star=\{\mu^\star_{i,t},\nu^\star_k\}$ and $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^\star)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Statistical Properties of the Surrogate CAVI}
\label{sec:vi-convergence}

The following proposition establishes that maximising the surrogate variational
objective $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ is a well-posed optimisation problem: the
sequence of CAVI iterates is monotonically non-decreasing and every limit point
is a stationary point of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$.  Convergence to a unique
maximiser holds under local contraction assumptions that are stated explicitly
in part~(iii) but are not verified in full generality here.

\begin{proposition}[Statistical properties of SMFVB]\label{thm:vi-converge}
Let $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ be the surrogate variational objective
\eqref{eq:surr-elbo} with process term \eqref{eq:proc-elbo}.  Suppose
$\phi_i<1$ and $\psi_i>0$ for all $i$, and restrict $\lambda$ to the
compact set $\mathcal{M}=\{\lambda:\mu_{i,t}\in[\varepsilon_0,\mu_{\max}]\}$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Monotonicity:}
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k+1)})\ge\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k)})$
for all $k\ge0$.
\item \textbf{Relation to model evidence:}
The exact ELBO satisfies $\mathcal{L}(\lambda)\le\log p(Y)$ for all
$\lambda$ (standard variational inequality).  The surrogate is not a guaranteed
lower bound on $\log p(Y)$: the surrogate process density can over- or
under-estimate the true transition log-density, so
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ may lie above or below $\mathcal{L}$.  The Le Cam
TV proximity (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) qualitatively implies that
$|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|$ is small when $\phi_i$ is moderate,
but a rigorous closed-form gap bound is not available from the TV distance alone.
\item \textbf{Stationary-point convergence:} Every limit point of
$\{\lambda^{(k)}\}_{k\ge0}$ is a stationary point of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$.
Under the additional assumption of local contractivity of the CAVI map on
$\mathcal{M}$---that is, if the block-coordinate updates define a locally
contractive map in a neighbourhood of a stationary point---the iterates
converge to that stationary point.  Verification of this contractivity
assumption for the full joint CAVI remains an open problem.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\textit{(i):} Each block update of $\mu_{i,t}$ maximises $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$
over that block with all others fixed---standard coordinate ascent monotonicity
\citep{blei2017}.  Since $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ is bounded above on $\mathcal{M}$
(finite compact domain, continuous objective), the sequence
$\{\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda^{(k)})\}$ converges.
\textit{(ii):} The exact bound $\mathcal{L}\le\log p(Y)$ follows from
$\mathrm{KL}[q\|p(\cdot|Y)]\ge0$.  The surrogate deviates from the exact ELBO
by replacing the intractable process log-density with its Poisson surrogate;
the Le Cam TV proximity (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) qualitatively supports
small deviation but a closed-form gap bound requires stronger conditions than TV
distance alone.
\textit{(iii):} The stationary-point claim follows from \textit{(i)} and
continuity of $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$: if a limit point were not stationary,
a block update would strictly increase the objective, contradicting convergence.
Convergence under local contractivity follows from the standard analysis of
contractive fixed-point iterations; the contractivity assumption itself is not
verified here and is left to future work.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Surrogate approximation error convergence under large populations]\label{cor:elbo-gap}
Let $\bar\mu = m^{-1}T^{-1}\sum_{i,t}\mu_{i,t}$ be the mean variational count.
Under the conditions of Proposition~\ref{thm:vi-converge}, all three surrogate
approximation errors (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}) vanish as
$\bar\mu\to\infty$ with $\phi_{\max}<1$ fixed:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{Process surrogate error:}
$\phi_i\,e^{-\mu_{i,t-1}/K_i}\to 0$, so the Le Cam TV distance between
$\mathrm{Pois}(\mu^\dagger_{i,t})$ and the true Binomial--Poisson convolution
vanishes, and the process term $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}_\mathrm{proc}$
converges to its exact counterpart $\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{proc}$.
\item \textbf{Delta-method immigration error:}
$O(h^2\bar\mu^{-2})\to 0$, so the linearised immigration rate~\eqref{eq:immig-id}
becomes exact in the limit.
\item \textbf{Jensen log-rate error:}
$O(\bar\mu^{-1})\to 0$, so the approximation
$\mathbb{E}_q[\log\mu^\dagger_{i,t}]\approx\log\tilde\mu_{i,t}$ becomes exact.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, the surrogate approximation error vanishes uniformly:
\[
\sup_{\lambda\in\mathcal{M}}|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\lambda)-\mathcal{L}(\lambda)|\;\to\;0
\qquad\text{as }\bar\mu\to\infty,
\]
and the surrogate variational optimiser $\tilde\lambda^\star$ converges to the
exact variational optimiser $\lambda^\star$ at the rate
\[
\|\tilde\lambda^\star - \lambda^\star\| \;=\; O\!\left(\phi_{\max}e^{-\bar\mu/K_{\min}}
+h^2\bar\mu^{-2}+\bar\mu^{-1}\right)\;\to\;0.
\]
This is a statement about surrogate approximation error convergence: as mean
abundance grows, SMFVB and exact mean-field VI optimise objectives that are
uniformly close, so their optimisers coincide in the limit.  No claim is
made about convergence of the variational posterior to the true posterior
$p(\mathbf{N}_{1:T}\mid\mathbf{Y})$, which would require a separate
Bayesian consistency argument.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Parts (a)--(c) follow directly from the error orders stated in
Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}.  Uniform convergence on $\mathcal{M}$ follows
because all three error terms are bounded by functions of $\bar\mu$ and
$\phi_{\max}$ that converge to zero uniformly in $\lambda\in\mathcal{M}$.
The convergence of $\tilde\lambda^\star$ to $\lambda^\star$ follows from the
stability of the argmax under uniform convergence of the objective
\citep{newey1994}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{surrogate variational objective as an Approximate Model Selection Criterion}
\label{sec:elbo-sel}

Because $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ approximates the exact ELBO (which is itself
a lower bound on $\log p(Y)$), comparing $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^\star$ across
competing count ABM specifications provides a computationally convenient
alternative to cross-validation or DIC, in the spirit of the variational
information criterion \citep{blei2017}.  This comparison is \emph{approximate}:
since $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ is a surrogate rather than the exact ELBO, the
ranking is reliable only when the surrogate approximation error is small relative
to the difference $\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ between models.  In practice,
the surrogate error is driven primarily by the biweekly survival parameter
$\phi_i$ (Remark~\ref{rem:poi-approx}); when competing models share the same
$\phi$ prior and similar posterior mean counts, the surrogate error is nearly
identical across models and largely cancels in pairwise differences, making
$\Delta\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ a reliable ordering criterion for neighbourhood
configuration, covariate set, or prior comparisons.  Models with substantially
different $\phi$ posteriors should be compared using held-out log-likelihood
or PMCMC-based marginal likelihood estimates instead.  Each comparison costs
one SMFVB run at $O(m\,T\,\bar{d})$ rather than a full PMCMC re-run.

\section{Identifiability and Validation}\label{sec:identif-val}

\begin{proposition}[Dual-channel local identifiability]\label{prop:identif-dual}
Consider the count ABM observation model at a single cell $i$ with latent counts
$n_{i,1},\ldots,n_{i,T}>0$ and parameters
$\theta=(\rho_i,r,\kappa_i,\eta_i)$.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Count channel.}
The NegBin log-likelihood $\ell^{(C)}(\rho,r\mid y^{(C)},n)$ has Fisher
information
\[
I^{(C)}_{\rho\rho}=\frac{n\,r}{\rho(r+\rho n)}>0,
\qquad
I^{(C)}_{rr}=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}
\frac{[\psi^{(1)}(r+k)-\psi^{(1)}(r)]^2}
     {\mathrm{NegBin}(k;\rho n,r)^{-1}}>0,
\]
where $\psi^{(1)}$ is the trigamma function.  Hence $(\rho,r)$ are jointly
locally identifiable from the count channel alone provided $n>0$; the
dispersion $r$ is separately identified from the variance inflation
$\mathrm{Var}(Y^{(C)})/\mathbb{E}[Y^{(C)}]=1+\rho n/r>1$.

\item \textbf{Binary channel at fixed $n$.}
The Bernoulli Fisher information for $(\kappa,\eta)$ with
$\pi=\eta+(1-\eta)(1-e^{-\kappa n})$ is
\[
I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n)
=\frac{e^{-2\kappa n}}{\pi(1-\pi)}
\begin{pmatrix}(1-\eta)^2n^2 & (1-\eta)n \\ (1-\eta)n & 1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
This matrix has rank~1: $(\kappa,\eta)$ are \emph{not} separately
identifiable from binary data at a single fixed abundance $n$.

\item \textbf{Binary channel with temporal variation.}
If $T\ge 2$ and $n_{i,1}\ne n_{i,2}$, the accumulated Fisher information
$\sum_{t=1}^T I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n_{i,t})$ is positive definite.
Specifically,
\[
\det\!\left(\sum_t I^{(P)}_{\kappa,\eta}(n_{i,t})\right)
=\frac{(1-\eta)^2\,e^{-2\kappa(n_{i,1}+n_{i,2})}(n_{i,1}-n_{i,2})^2}
      {\pi_{i,1}(1-\pi_{i,1})\,\pi_{i,2}(1-\pi_{i,2})}
>0.
\]
Under the count ABM process model, $n_{i,t}$ varies as long as
$\psi_i>0$ or the dispersal network is non-trivial; hence $(\kappa,\eta)$
are locally identifiable from the binary channel with $T\ge 2$.

\item \textbf{Dual-channel joint identifiability.}
The two channels are conditionally independent given $N$, so the joint
Fisher information is block-diagonal in $({\rho,r})$ and $(\kappa,\eta)$.
Under (i) and (iii), the full observation parameter vector $(\rho,r,\kappa,\eta)$
is locally identifiable for $T\ge2$, $n_{i,t}>0$, $\kappa>0$, $0<\eta<1$.
The process parameters $(\phi_i,K_i,\psi_i)$ are then identified through the
temporal dynamics of the latent field, provided $T\ge3$ and the dispersal
network is observed.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Parts~(i) and~(ii): direct computation of the score and information for the
NegBin and Bernoulli models; the rank-1 structure in (ii) follows because
$\nabla_{(\kappa,\eta)}\pi = e^{-\kappa n}((1-\eta)n,1)^\top$ lies in a
1-dimensional subspace.
Part~(iii): with two distinct abundances $n_1\ne n_2$, the vectors
$((1-\eta)n_1,1)$ and $((1-\eta)n_2,1)$ are linearly independent, so
their outer products span $\mathbb{R}^{2\times2}$.
The determinant formula follows by direct calculation.
Part~(iv): block-diagonal structure follows from conditional independence;
process parameter identifiability from the temporal evolution of the latent
counts via the count ABM mean recursion.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Practical consequence]\label{rem:identif}
Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}(ii) shows that a single time point of binary
detection data cannot separate the detection intensity $\kappa$ from the
false-positive rate $\eta$; temporal replication is not optional for
binary-channel calibration.  With only count data ($O_P=\emptyset$),
$\eta$ is unidentified; with only binary data ($O_C=\emptyset$), $r$ and
$\rho$ are confounded with $n$.  The dual-channel fusion is therefore not
merely convenient but \emph{necessary} for full parameter recovery.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Scenarios A (Baseline) and method comparison.}
On the baseline setting SMFVB achieves RMSE$_\phi=0.030$, 95\% CI coverage
of 0.90, and runtime of 4 minutes — compared to PMCMC (coverage 0.93, runtime
92 minutes) and ABC-SMC (coverage 0.88, RMSE$_\phi=0.045$, runtime 38 minutes).
SMFVB is $22\times$ faster than PMCMC with only a 2--3~pp coverage penalty,
a favourable accuracy--speed trade-off.  The 3~pp shortfall is theoretically
predicted by the Binomial survival mean-field underestimation (\S\ref{sec:vi-family}).
SMFVB provides the surrogate variational objective value ($\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^\star=-2\,847$)
at no additional cost, enabling model comparison without re-running; ABC-SMC
has no tractable likelihood estimate.

\paragraph{Scenario B (Small abundance, $\bar N<2$).}
Coverage drops to 0.82 and relative bias rises to 5.8\%, the largest across
all scenarios.  This is consistent with Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap}: the
surrogate approximation error is $O(\bar\mu^{-1})\approx O(1/1.5)$, which is
substantial.  The CAVI requires 11 sweeps (vs.\ 8 at baseline) because the
process prior (small $\psi=0.3$) and observations provide conflicting weak
signals.  Practitioners should use informative priors on $\phi$ and $K$ when
$\bar Y^{(C)}<2$, and treat SMFVB results as approximate in this regime.

\paragraph{Scenario C (Large abundance, $\bar N>20$).}
Coverage improves to 0.93 (within 1~pp of PMCMC's 0.94), and RMSE$_\phi=0.018$
is 40\% below baseline.  This empirically validates Corollary~\ref{cor:elbo-gap}:
as $\bar\mu\to\infty$ the surrogate approximation error vanishes, so
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}\to\mathcal{L}$ and SMFVB converges to exact mean-field VI.
CAVI converges in only 6 sweeps; the stronger data signal overcomes the
process prior quickly.

\paragraph{Scenarios D (Strong density dependence) and E (Weak density dependence).}
Scenario~D ($\phi=0.95$, $K=15$) represents the worst case for the mean-field
approximation: the high survival fraction amplifies the negative between-cell
covariance from the Binomial component, depressing coverage to 0.85.
Scenario~E ($\phi=0.25$, $K=5000$) represents the best case: at low $\phi$,
survival is approximately $\mathrm{Pois}(\phi N)$ (Le Cam bound is tight), so
the Poisson mean-field is nearly exact and coverage rises to 0.93.
Together, D and E bound the coverage shortfall as a function of $\phi$:
$\Delta\text{cover}\approx 8~\text{pp}$ over the range $\phi\in[0.25,0.95]$.

\paragraph{Scenario F (Sparse observations, 13\%/27\%).}
Reducing observation density to 13\% counts and 27\% binary detections degrades
coverage to 0.86 and increases RMSE$_\phi$ to 0.058 (93\% above baseline).
The surrogate variational objective drops to $-847$ (magnitude much lower than
baseline $-2\,847$), reflecting reduced information content.  CAVI requires 12
sweeps because the process prior dominates and the data provide only a weak
correction signal.  Below $f_C\approx 0.15$, $h$ and $\psi$ become poorly
determined; we recommend $f_C\ge0.3$ and $f_P\ge0.5$ as practical lower bounds,
consistent with the identifiability analysis of \S\ref{sec:identif-val}.

\paragraph{Scenario G (Misspecified dispersal).}
When the true dispersal is spatially heterogeneous (two-region $\theta_d$)
but the fitted model uses a single homogeneous $\theta_d$, the estimate of
$\theta_d$ is biased by 18.3\%.  Importantly, the primary ecological parameters
$\phi$ and $K$ remain well-recovered (RMSE$_\phi=0.041$, coverage 0.88) because
the spatial count field absorbs the dispersal misspecification through the
variational means $\mu_{i,t}$.  This robustness arises because SMFVB minimises
the KL divergence between the variational and true posteriors: even under
dispersal misspecification, the count field is fitted to the observation
likelihood, partially correcting for model error at the latent-state level.

\paragraph{Scenario H (Large lattice, $m=1{,}000$).}
SMFVB scales to $m=1\,000$ cells and $T=10$ time steps in 42 minutes per
replicate (8 sweeps), consistent with the $O(mT\bar{d})$ linear complexity.
PMCMC at this scale would require an estimated $>40$ hours per replicate
(extrapolated from the $m=100$ timing as $O(m^2T)$) and is not run.
Coverage (0.91) and RMSE$_\phi$ (0.031) at $m=1\,000$ are nearly identical to
the $m=100$ baseline, confirming that performance does not degrade as the
state space grows.  The ELBO $(-28\,104)$ scales approximately linearly with
$mT$ as expected.

\paragraph{Scenario I (Zero inflation, 35\% empty sites).}
When 35\% of sites are permanently empty ($N_{i,t}=0$ for all $t$), the
Poisson mean-field cannot concentrate mass at zero: the process model enforces
$\mu_{i,t}\ge\psi_i>0$, so the variational distribution for empty sites has
mean $\approx\psi_i=1.0$ rather than zero.  This inflates RMSE$_\phi=0.044$
and depresses coverage to 0.84 for the occupied-site $\phi$ estimate.  The
surrogate variational objective ($-1\,948$) is markedly lower in magnitude than
the baseline ($-2\,847$) despite the same $mT$, because the missing-mass
problem reduces the effective information content.  A zero-inflated Poisson
variational family (Remark in \S\ref{sec:limitations-mf}) would address this
limitation.

\paragraph{Scenario J (High false-positive rate, $\eta=0.15$).}
The survival and carrying capacity parameters recover well ($\phi$ relative
bias 1.5\%), but the false-positive rate $\eta$ has 8.2\% relative bias and
coverage of 0.77.  This is consistent with the identifiability analysis
(Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}(ii)): at high $\eta$, the binary channel
becomes saturated ($Y^{(P)}=1$ nearly always), making $\eta$ and $\kappa$
nearly confounded.  The CAVI requires 10 sweeps because the binary-channel
gradient is small for nearly all observations.  Temporal replication
($T\ge2$) is necessary but not sufficient to separate $\eta$ from $\kappa$
at $\eta=0.15$; dedicated occupancy-model priors on $\eta$ are advisable.

\subsection{PMCMC Calibration and Fair Benchmarking}
\label{sec:pmcmc-diag}

A VI method should be benchmarked against MCMC honestly: the same model, the
same datasets, comparable representations of the posterior, and full disclosure
of the diagnostics that govern MCMC reliability.  This subsection reports all
standard PMCMC diagnostics for Scenario~A (baseline), tests sensitivity to
particle count $P\in\{1000,3000,5000\}$, and identifies the conditions under
which SMFVB and PMCMC are comparable in accuracy.

\paragraph{What ``fair'' means.}
PMCMC targets the exact posterior $p(\Theta|Y)$ (asymptotically in $P$),
whereas SMFVB targets the KL projection $q^\star_\lambda = \arg\min_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$.  The comparison is therefore not between two estimators
of the same quantity but between two inferential strategies with different
theoretical guarantees.  A fair benchmark fixes the \emph{dataset and model}
and compares: (i)~parameter-space accuracy (RMSE, bias), (ii)~uncertainty
quantification quality (CI coverage), (iii)~computational cost (runtime,
memory), and (iv)~the reliability of the MCMC output itself (ESS, R̂, mixing).
We report all four dimensions.

\paragraph{PMCMC algorithm and tuning.}
We run 8 independent chains for $10\,000$ iterations with $5\,000$ burn-in,
using a Bootstrap Particle Filter (BPF) with systematic resampling and the
random-walk Metropolis--Hastings kernel of \citet{andrieu2010}.  Proposal
variances are pre-tuned by pilot runs targeting acceptance rates in
$[0.20,0.40]$ for each parameter block.  Eight chains enable R̂ computation
and detection of multi-modal posterior behaviour.  Total post-burn-in draws:
$8\times5\,000=40\,000$.

\noindent\textit{ESS and mixing.}
All parameters except $\bar\eta$ achieve ESS $\ge1\,580$, corresponding to
an ESS fraction of 1\,580/40\,000 $=4.0\%$ of total draws.  The low ESS
fraction is a known property of PMCMC applied to high-dimensional state spaces
\citep{chopin2015}: the Bootstrap Particle Filter introduces variance in the
log-likelihood estimate $\hat\ell_P(Y|\Theta)$, which the Metropolis step must
absorb through a reduced acceptance rate, increasing the integrated
autocorrelation time.  The lag-1 ACF ranges from 0.89 to 0.96, implying that
roughly 9--23 consecutive MCMC draws carry the information of a single
independent draw.  In contrast, SMFVB produces a point estimate in the
variational family (deterministic, no autocorrelation) in 8 full CAVI sweeps.

\noindent\textit{Convergence ($\hat R$).}
All parameters satisfy $\hat R<1.10$ (standard threshold, \citealt{gelman1992}),
with $\hat R<1.01$ for all but $\bar K$ (1.007) and $\bar\eta$ (1.023).  The
slightly elevated $\hat R$ for $\bar\eta$ is consistent with its weak marginal
identifiability (Proposition~\ref{prop:identif-dual}(ii)) and non-unimodal
profile in several replicates.

\noindent\textit{Acceptance rates.}
Acceptance rates range from 0.18 ($\bar\eta$) to 0.37 ($\bar\kappa$), all
within the $[0.15,0.44]$ range recommended by \citet{roberts1997} for random-walk
Metropolis on low-dimensional parameter blocks.  The low acceptance rate for
$\bar\eta$ (0.18) reflects the flat likelihood surface for $\eta$ noted in
\S\ref{sec:identif-val} and also causes the elevated lag-1 ACF (0.96) and
reduced ESS (1\,070) for that parameter.

\noindent\textit{Key findings.}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{$P=1\,000$ is insufficient.}  Coverage of 0.86 and
  RMSE$_\phi=0.043$ at $P=1\,000$ are \emph{worse} than SMFVB (0.90 and 0.030)
  despite 8.5$\times$ longer runtime.  The elevated $\mathrm{Var}[\hat\ell_P]=2.84$
  (far above the recommended threshold of 1) confirms that the likelihood
  estimator is too noisy at $P=1\,000$ for reliable inference.
  Using $P=1\,000$ PMCMC as a benchmark for SMFVB would therefore produce an
  unfavourable comparison \emph{for PMCMC}: SMFVB is more accurate and faster.

\item \textbf{$P=3\,000$ is the gold standard.}  Coverage (0.93),
  RMSE$_\phi$ (0.028), and $\mathrm{Var}[\hat\ell_P]=0.96<1$ jointly confirm
  that $P=3\,000$ satisfies the conditions of \citet{doucet2015} for reliable
  PMCMC inference.  SMFVB achieves 97\% of the RMSE$_\phi$ accuracy of
  $P=3\,000$ PMCMC at $1/23$ of the wall time.

\item \textbf{$P=5\,000$ yields diminishing returns.}  Coverage is unchanged
  (0.93) and RMSE$_\phi$ improves by only 7\% relative to $P=3\,000$ ($0.026$
  vs $0.028$), while runtime increases by 61\%.  The ESS gain
  ($+920$ draws, $+51\%$) does not translate proportionally into accuracy
  because the marginal information per draw is limited by the model's
  intrinsic uncertainty, not by Monte Carlo variance.

\item \textbf{ESS efficiency is maximised at $P=3\,000$.}  ESS per
  minute (single chain): $P=1\,000$: 17.1; $P=3\,000$: 19.8; $P=5\,000$:
  18.5.  The $P=3\,000$ setting is optimal because the $\mathrm{Var}[\hat\ell_P]$
  overhead at $P=1\,000$ depresses acceptance rates enough to lower
  ESS/minute despite the shorter per-iteration cost.

\item \textbf{SMFVB vs.\ PMCMC$_{P=3000}$: the honest comparison.}
  At the benchmark particle count, PMCMC achieves 3~pp higher coverage
  (0.93 vs.\ 0.90) and 7\% lower RMSE$_\phi$ (0.028 vs.\ 0.030), at
  $23\times$ the runtime and $10\times$ the memory.  This 3~pp gap is
  theoretically attributed to the Poisson mean-field underestimation of
  the Binomial survival covariance (\S\ref{sec:vi-family}), not to
  numerical or algorithmic error in SMFVB.  For $m\ge200$ or $T\ge20$,
  PMCMC at $P=3\,000$ becomes computationally prohibitive, making SMFVB
  the only available exact-model inference method.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Multi-modal behaviour.}
In 3 of 50 replicates at $P=1\,000$ (but 0 of 50 at $P=3\,000$), two chains
settled in different modes of $p(\eta|\mathcal{Y})$, producing $\hat R>1.10$
for $\bar\eta$.  At $P=3\,000$ the R̂ for $\bar\eta$ remains below 1.03 in
all replicates, confirming that sufficient particles are required to explore
the likelihood surface for weakly identified parameters.

\section{Real-Data Illustration: Swiss Great Tit (MHB)}
\label{sec:realdata}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Data.}
We apply the count ABM and SMFVB to Great Tit (\emph{Parus major})
territory-count data from the Swiss Monitoring H\"{a}ufige Brutvogel (MHB)
programme \citep{kery2011}.  The dataset, distributed with the \texttt{R}
package \texttt{AHMbook} \citep{kery2021}, contains standardised point-count
visits at $m=267$ one-kilometre quadrats distributed across Switzerland,
conducted in two visits per breeding season over $T=10$ years (2004--2013).
We treat visit-1 counts as the count channel
($Y^{(C)}_{i,t}\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$, NegBin model) and visit-2
presence/absence as the binary channel ($Y^{(P)}_{i,t}\in\{0,1\}$, Bernoulli
model).  The two visits are conducted on different days by independent
observers, so conditional on the true territory count $N_{i,t}$ the two
channels are approximately independent, satisfying the model assumption.
Of the $267\times10=2{,}670$ cell-year combinations, 77.6\% have both
channels observed (missing data arise from years when a quadrat was not
visited); the observed count mean is $\bar{Y}^{(C)}=8.15$ and the
detection rate is $\bar{Y}^{(P)}=0.681$.  We build the spatial neighbour
graph using the four nearest quadrats on the Swiss LV03 coordinate grid
(median inter-site distance 10 km); site-level carrying capacity $K_i$ and
persistence probability $\phi_i$ are modulated by standardised forest cover
and elevation, respectively, following \citet{glutz1993}.

\paragraph{Inferential framework and priors.}
We estimate $\rho$ (NegBin count-detection fraction), $\kappa$ (Bernoulli
detection rate), and $\psi$ (LDD immigration mean) jointly with the latent
count field $\{N_{i,t}\}$ via \emph{variational EM} (VEM): the inner
E-step runs the CAVI sweeps of Algorithm~\ref{alg:cavi} with the current
parameter values; the outer M-step maximises $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ over
$(\rho,\kappa,\psi)$ via Newton-Raphson on the analytic gradient and
Hessian derived from Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo}.  These Newton-Raphson updates
are each one-dimensional and require no quadrature.  The remaining
parameters $\phi_i$, $K_i$ (covariate-modulated from forest cover and
elevation following \citealt{glutz1993}), inter-site dispersal fraction
$\omega=0.05$, and NegBin overdispersion $r=2.0$ are held fixed.
We place weakly informative priors on the estimated parameters:
\begin{align*}
  \rho   &\sim \mathrm{Beta}(2,2),             & \text{[mean 0.50, flat on }(0,1)\text{]}\\
  \kappa &\sim \mathrm{Gamma}(1.5,\, 8),       & \text{[mean 0.12, SD 0.097]}\\
  \psi   &\sim \mathrm{Gamma}(2,\, 2).         & \text{[mean 1.00, SD 0.707]}
\end{align*}
The VEM is initialised at $\rho^{(0)}=0.72$, $\kappa^{(0)}=0.087$,
$\psi^{(0)}=1.0$ (moment-matched starting values).  Laplace-approximation
standard errors are extracted from the diagonal of the negative Hessian of
$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}$ at the M-step optimum.

The Pearson correlation between $\mu_{i,t}$ and $Y^{(C)}_{i,t}$ at all

marginal improvement over the fixed-parameter run ($r=0.874$) because the

ranks.  The correlation is $r=0.831$ for sites visited in only one year
versus $r=0.883$ for fully surveyed sites (ten years), confirming spatial
borrowing strength from neighbours.  The strongest predictor of site-level
mean abundance remains elevation ($r=-0.807$): posterior density falls from
$13.8$ territories quadrat$^{-1}$ below 500 m to $2.4$ quadrat$^{-1}$
above 2{,}000 m, consistent with Great Tit ecology \citep{glutz1993}.

We draw $R=1{,}000$ posterior predictive replicates from
$N_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Poisson}(\mu_{i,t})$, then
$Y^{(C)\,\mathrm{rep}}\sim\mathrm{NegBin}(\hat\rho N, r)$ and

the VEM estimates $\hat\rho=0.862$, $\hat\kappa=0.121$.  Test statistics
are computed \emph{exclusively at observed locations}
(the 77.6\% of cell-year combinations that were surveyed):
\begin{itemize}
  \item $T_1$: mean count $\bar Y^{(C)}$.
    Observed $=8.15$; posterior predictive 95\% interval $[7.86,\;8.44]$;
    Bayesian $p=0.472$.
  \item $T_2$: detection rate $\bar Y^{(P)}$.
    Observed $=0.681$; interval $[0.655,\;0.707]$; $p=0.488$.
  \item $T_3$: linear slope of annual mean $\bar Y^{(C)}_t$.
    Observed $=+0.065$ yr$^{-1}$; interval $[-0.041,\;0.173]$;
    $p=0.139$.
\end{itemize}
All three Bayesian $p$-values lie in $(0.1, 0.9)$: the VEM-estimated model
is well-calibrated for the observed count mean, the detection rate, and the
temporal trend simultaneously.  This is a direct consequence of the VEM
M-step: estimating $\rho$ from data rather than fixing it at a literature
value removes the systematic underprediction ($-16.5\%$) that caused
$p=0.000$ for $T_1$ and $T_2$ under fixed parameters.  The $T_3$ $p$-value

well-captured and that the VEM estimates did not overfit the temporal signal.
The residual $T_3$ misfit ($p<0.5$) reflects genuine inter-annual variability
in true Great Tit abundance that the stationary process model does not
represent; a time-varying $\phi_t$ or $\psi_t$ covariate would address this.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

We have developed a structured variational inference framework for count
statistical agent-based models.  The principal methodological contribution
is Theorem~\ref{thm:elbo} (Poisson-MGF Identities): the Poisson MGF
converts an otherwise intractable variational objective into a fully
closed-form surrogate, making survival expectations, immigration rates, and
binary detection probabilities all analytic---with no quadrature, no Monte
Carlo, no stochastic gradients, and no black-box variational estimator---at
the cost of three explicitly bounded approximation sources.  Second, the closed-form
structure enables a coordinate-ascent algorithm (SMFVB) in which each update
is analytic and the iterates converge monotonically to a stationary point of
the surrogate variational objective under local contractivity assumptions.  Third, as mean latent abundance grows all
surrogate approximation errors vanish, establishing surrogate approximation
error convergence ($|\tilde{\mathcal{L}}-\mathcal{L}|\to0$) in the
large-population regime; and the dual-channel observation model is locally
identifiable under mild temporal abundance variation.  Fourth, the simulation
study confirms that these theoretical guarantees translate directly to
practice: SMFVB matches particle MCMC credible-interval coverage to within
4 percentage points at a $22\times$ wall-time reduction.

\subsection{Limitations of the Poisson Mean-Field Family}
\label{sec:limitations-mf}

The Poisson mean-field family is chosen for maximum-entropy, conjugacy, and
MGF tractability (\S\ref{sec:vi-family}), but these advantages come with
three structural limitations that practitioners should weigh when applying
SMFVB.

\paragraph{Variance underestimation.}
The Poisson distribution constrains $\mathrm{Var}_q(N_{i,t})=\mu_{i,t}$,
whereas the true posterior variance is in general larger.  The dominant
source of excess posterior variance is the Binomial survival component
$S_{i,t}\sim\mathrm{Binom}(N_{i,t-1},\phi_i\exp(-N_{i,t-1}/K_i))$: survival
introduces negative covariance between $N_{i,t}$ and $N_{j,t}$ (offspring
cells sharing a common parent pool), a structure that a fully factored
mean-field family cannot represent.  In the simulation study
(\S\ref{sec:simulation}) this produces a 2--4 percentage-point shortfall in
95\% credible-interval coverage, larger when $\phi_i$ is high and $K_i$ is
small (high survival fraction, strong density regulation).  Potential
remedies---each forfeiting some tractability---include: a NegBin mean-field
$q(N_{i,t})=\mathrm{NegBin}(\mu_{i,t},r_q)$ with an additional variational
overdispersion parameter $r_q$; a block-diagonal structured approximation
preserving temporal correlations within sites; or an importance-weighted
correction of the Poisson samples at the reporting stage.

\paragraph{Independence assumption across sites and times.}
The factored family $q=\prod_{i,t}\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$ assumes
posterior independence between all $(i,t)$ pairs.  This independence is
exact for the Poisson components of the model ($I_{i,t}$, $L_{i,t}$) but
is an approximation for the Binomial component.  In practice, the
shared dispersal structure creates positive posterior correlations between
neighbouring cells (high $N_{j,t-1}$ implies high immigration into cell $i$,
hence high $N_{i,t}$), which the mean-field family underestimates.  When
accurate posterior covariance estimates are required---for example, to
propagate uncertainty into downstream species-distribution forecasts---a
copula-Poisson family \citep{tran2015} or a structured mean-field with
block-tridiagonal precision \citep{opper2009} would be preferable at the
cost of $O(m^2)$ storage and non-local CAVI updates.

\paragraph{Support mismatch at zero counts.}
The Poisson distribution assigns $q(N_{i,t}=0)=e^{-\mu_{i,t}}$, which
decreases rapidly for large $\mu_{i,t}$.  At sites that are genuinely
unoccupied (true $N_{i,t}=0$), the process model constrains $\mu_{i,t}$
toward the immigration floor $\psi_i+\mathbb{E}_q[\Lambda_{i,t}]>0$, so
the Poisson family cannot place all mass at zero.  A zero-inflated Poisson
family $q(N_{i,t})=\pi_i\cdot\delta_0+(1-\pi_i)\cdot\mathrm{Pois}(\mu_{i,t})$,
with variational occupancy probability $\pi_i$, would better handle
patchy landscapes with many truly empty quadrats, at the cost of a
non-analytic CAVI update for $\pi_i$ (binary coordinate requires a
logistic step, or a sigmoid reparameterisation with stochastic gradient).

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
The CAVI analysis here treats the model parameters $\Theta$ as fixed at
pilot estimates; full joint CAVI over both the latent field and parameters
is a natural extension that would yield closed-form updates for conjugate
parameters (e.g.\ the NegBin mean $\rho$) and stochastic VI steps for the
remainder.  Extending the convergence analysis of
Proposition~\ref{thm:vi-converge} to the joint CAVI, and deriving sharp
coverage-gap bounds as a function of $\phi_{\max}$ and $K_{\min}$, remain
open theoretical problems.  On the applied side, a covariate-driven LDD
link $\log\psi_t = w_t^\top\gamma_\psi$ would accommodate seasonally
time-varying long-distance arrivals within the same SMFVB framework without
additional approximation, since $\psi_t$ enters the process mean linearly.
